## TL;DR
Akram Baharlouei, a machine learning engineer at Altos Labs, explains why single-cell biology matters for cellular rejuvenation and drug development, walks through how single-cell data is measured (primarily via RNA-seq), and reviews the current state of foundation models—finding that transformer-based approaches often underperform simple baselines, while flow matching models like PrimeFlow show more promise by directly predicting data distributions.

## Key Takeaways
- Single-cell biology is central to Altos Labs' mission of restoring cell health and resilience through cellular rejuvenation, building on Yamanaka's 2006 discovery of reprogramming factors.
- The field aspires toward "virtual cell," "virtual tissue," and ultimately "virtual human" models that could dramatically accelerate drug development, which currently takes up to 10 years per drug with acceptance rates below 5%.
- RNA-seq (transcriptomics) is the dominant modality for foundation model training because it scales to tens of millions—or even billions—of cells, though other modalities like proteomics and morphology remain harder to measure at scale.
- Single-cell data is inherently noisy and heterogeneous due to both biological factors (burst-like transcriptional dynamics, cell cycle stages) and technical factors (lab-to-lab and machine-to-machine variability); measuring two identical cells does not yield identical readings.
- Transformer-based models (e.g., scGPT, Geneformer) treat cells as sentences and genes as tokens using BERT-style masked prediction, but comprehensive benchmarking shows they often fail to outperform simple linear models.
- The information bottleneck from compressing high-dimensional single-cell data into latent vectors loses critical biological signal, limiting transformer model effectiveness.
- Flow matching models such as PrimeFlow, which learn to match data distributions rather than compress into latents, appear to outperform autoregressive and autoencoder-based approaches.
- Scaling alone is insufficient—improving data quality and measurement fidelity to better reflect real organisms is equally important for future progress.
- Two NeurIPS benchmarking papers (on multimodal data and perturbation response modeling) provide community resources for evaluating these models.

## Detailed Breakdown

### Introduction and Altos Labs Mission [00:12](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-561cZmir5Q&t=12s)
Akram introduces himself as a machine learning engineer at Altos Labs, a biotech startup focused on restoring cell health and resilience through cellular rejuvenation, with the goal of reversing disease and disability associated with aging. He frames the talk as an engineering perspective from someone without a biology background, focusing on the challenges of building foundation models for single-cell biology.

### Why Single-Cell Matters: Yamanaka Factors and Reprogramming [01:13](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-561cZmir5Q&t=73s)
Akram uses the Yamanaka factor discovery as his motivating example. In 2006, Shinya Yamanaka identified four transcription factors that, when overexpressed in an old skin cell, could reprogram it back to an embryonic stem cell-like state. This earned Yamanaka the 2012 Nobel Prize and opened new possibilities in regenerative medicine. A key application is partial reprogramming—changing a cell's age without changing its type, potentially restoring youthful function. Akram notes that the first reprogramming medicine (OSK) is expected to enter human testing around 2026, roughly 20 years after the original discovery.

### The Vision: Virtual Cells, Tissues, and Humans [03:18](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-561cZmir5Q&t=198s)
The broader motivation for studying single cells is to build a unified, holistic model of cells, then tissues, then organs, and ultimately the entire human body. Projects like the Human Cell Atlas have begun mapping every cell type in the human body. The ultimate vision encompasses concepts like "virtual cell," "virtual tissue," "virtual human," and "digital twins"—all aimed at better understanding living organisms to improve medicine and drug development.

### Drug Development Challenges and AI's Potential Role [04:19](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-561cZmir5Q&t=259s)
Akram contrasts Moore's law with the declining rate of drug development, noting that despite technological advances, the number of drugs developed per year is decreasing. Drug development has an acceptance rate of roughly 5% or less, with pipelines taking up to 10 years and costing billions. While AI and virtual cell/organ/human models could reduce this timeline, Akram cautions that savings in early R&D (e.g., protein design) may not meaningfully shorten the entire pipeline without innovation across all stages.

### Measuring Single Cells: Modalities and Data Scale [05:52](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-561cZmir5Q&t=352s)
Single cells can be measured across multiple modalities: genome (DNA sequencing, largely identical across cells), transcriptome (RNA-seq, measuring gene expression profiles of ~20,000 genes per cell), proteome (protein measurement, biologically important but low-throughput and hard to scale), and morphology (cell shape and structure via microscopy, useful for spatial context). RNA-seq dominates foundation model training because PCR-based technologies make it scalable—datasets range from tens of millions to 500 million cells, with projects targeting 1 billion cells. However, Akram stresses that true biological understanding requires technological advances in other modalities as well.

### Data Quality Challenges: Noise, Heterogeneity, and Snapshots [08:26](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-561cZmir5Q&t=506s)
Even with billions of RNA-seq samples, the data poses significant challenges for foundation models. Measuring two identical cells does not produce identical readings due to both biological and technical noise. Biologically, cells undergo burst-like changes in transcription, cell division, and other dynamic processes; current sequencing captures only a snapshot from what is effectively a continuous movie. Technical variability across labs and machines further compounds the problem. Akram briefly notes that the high-dimensional, multi-state nature of the data might eventually make quantum computing a natural fit, but for now the focus is on current AI.

### Transformer-Based Foundation Models [10:30](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-561cZmir5Q&t=630s)
Akram reviews transformer-based models such as scGPT and Geneformer, which treat each cell as a sentence and genes as tokens, using BERT-style masked prediction to learn gene-gene relationships via bidirectional attention. Downstream tasks include cell type prediction and perturbation response modeling. However, the approach of compressing single-cell data into a latent vector loses substantial information. Comprehensive benchmarking (two NeurIPS papers) revealed that these expensive models sometimes perform on par with or worse than simple linear models, and they underperform relative to foundation models in language and imaging domains.

### Flow Matching Models: PrimeFlow [13:04](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-561cZmir5Q&t=784s)
Akram introduces flow matching-based models as a promising alternative. These models start from Gaussian noise and learn to match the data distribution directly, rather than compressing into latents. He highlights PrimeFlow (available on arXiv), which appears to outperform autoregressive and autoencoder-based models like CPADR. Visual results show PrimeFlow better matching ground truth distributions (green dots), whereas autoencoder-based models tend to predict means rather than capture full distributions. MMD (Maximum Mean Discrepancy) scores confirm the advantage.

### Key Takeaways and Conclusion [15:11](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-561cZmir5Q&t=911s)
Akram summarizes three main points: (1) single-cell biology is important for cellular rejuvenation, digital human modeling, and drug development; (2) RNA-seq is the most available and scalable modality but data quality and measurement fidelity must improve alongside scale; (3) flow matching models currently perform best for single-cell data, but massive scaling of both data quantity and quality is needed for models to generalize to unseen data. He references three papers: scGeneScope and a perturbation benchmarking paper (both at NeurIPS last year), and PrimeFlow.
