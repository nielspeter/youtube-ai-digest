## TL;DR
Sarah Sachs, who leads AI engineering teams at Notion, delivers a pragmatic talk on surviving "Token Town" — the expensive, opaque reality of building AI products in a market dominated by a few frontier labs. She argues that companies must stay model-agnostic, understand the true cost-per-capability-per-second trade-offs, leverage open-weight models and CPUs where possible, and build durable product value through data flywheels and orchestration rather than tying themselves to a single provider.

## Key Takeaways
- **Cost is the primary structural barrier** to scaling AI products; many companies commit to ambitious AI visions but cannot sustain the token economics.
- **Your model supplier is also your competitor.** Frontier labs sell first-party products and resell tokens at a surcharge, meaning companies that simply resell tokens are on the wrong end of a bad deal.
- **Stay model-agnostic.** Tying yourself to one lab in exchange for a discount means you lose frontier capabilities whenever another lab takes the lead, which happens frequently.
- **Not all traffic requires the most capable (and slowest) model.** Notion routes ~75% of traffic through an "auto model" and reserves frontier models only for genuinely complex tasks.
- **Evaluate trade-offs across entire trajectories**, not just single API calls. Notion's partnership with Parallel for web search was justified by looking at whole-search trajectories, not isolated latency or cost metrics.
- **Open-weight models are no longer just for small SFT tasks.** Models like Kimmy 26 and GLM 52 are credible alternatives that lower costs and provide negotiation leverage against the oligopoly.
- **Don't use LLMs for deterministic work.** Use CPUs and traditional code for tasks like converting CSVs to PDFs or running SQL queries — this is where companies waste tokens fastest.
- **Security is the next major challenge.** The "lethal trifecta" — access to private data, exposure to untrusted content, and external communication — creates escalating risk as systems become more autonomous.
- **Multi-agent orchestration is the core of the software factory vision.** Notion's internal workflows coordinate agents from different labs (Claude, Decagon, Codex) within active documents, avoiding lab lock-in.

## Detailed Breakdown

### Introduction and the AI Transformation Journey [01:03](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-I5W5QVAT8E&t=63s)
Sarah Sachs introduces herself as the leader of Notion's AI engineering teams, jokingly comparing herself to "Anna Wintour" because she negotiates AI contracts for a living. She frames Notion's evolution: it has always been a durable system of record for collaboration, but today that collaboration includes humans and agents. She outlines a maturity model — AI as a thought partner, AI as an assistant, AI as a teammate, and AI as a critical system — and notes that 88% of companies can't get past the assistant stage, largely due to siloed data and the lack of a durable system of record.

### The Cost Problem in Token Town [04:46](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-I5W5QVAT8E&t=286s)
Sachs presents cost as the dominant structural barrier to building AI products at scale. She illustrates with real-world scenarios: a reasoning model upgrade that triples output tokens while keeping per-token pricing the same, and a new model generation that costs 40% more while its predecessor faces deprecation. She asks the audience whether their revenue is growing 3x or 40% in the same period — it isn't. Fortune 500 companies can hire consulting teams to navigate this; everyone else is stuck without leverage.

### Your Supplier Is Your Competitor [07:26](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-I5W5QVAT8E&t=446s)
This is one of the talk's core messages. Frontier labs sell first-party AI products and also sell tokens to third parties at a surcharge, who then resell them at another surcharge. Sachs argues this is structurally indefensible. If you tie yourself to one provider, you have no exit. She references analysis (attributed to Dylan) showing the large gap between what labs charge for first-party products versus what they sell tokens for. The winning strategy is not to compete on token economics but to build data flywheels, understand customer needs, and create compelling UI and orchestration that justify the cost.

### Cost per Capability per Second [09:30](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-I5W5QVAT8E&t=570s)
Sachs references a Citadel memo about a bifurcation between frontier and everyday usage. She argues that simpler models are often the most cost-effective for productivity augmentation. Companies need to develop expertise on where the boundary lies for their own product — what counts as "frontier" versus "everyday." Triage of an email inbox, for example, should not run on the most expensive model. She also explains that model pricing is incentivized like gas stations: the best model prices however it wants, and the second-best just needs to be slightly cheaper. Price does not correlate with capability growth.

### The Model-Agnostic Playbook [13:05](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-I5W5QVAT8E&t=785s)
Sachs outlines Notion's "Switzerland approach." Notion's auto model handles roughly 75% of traffic, with state-of-the-art frontier models available for the rest. She offers a concrete playbook: build for multimodal and model interoperability from the start; evaluate cost per capability per second across entire trajectories (not single calls); switch models fast and often; and share eval expertise with frontier labs as a bargaining chip instead of accepting large commits that lock you in. She cites Notion's partnership with Parallel for web search as an example where trajectory-level evaluation justified the choice even though single-call metrics weren't obviously cheapest.

### Open-Weight Models and CPUs [15:07](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-I5W5QVAT8E&t=907s)
Open-weight models have matured significantly. Sachs cites Kimmy 26 and GLM 52 as models that now outperform some frontier offerings and serve as credible alternatives, applying downward pricing pressure on the oligopoly. She advises companies to prepare now because today's frontier tasks will likely be covered by open weights in six months. Additionally, she argues that many jobs don't need GPUs or LLMs at all — converting CSVs to PDFs, making tool calls via CLI, or running deterministic SQL queries should use CPUs and traditional code. This is where companies become "token poor" fastest.

### Security and the Lethal Trifecta [18:14](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-I5W5QVAT8E&t=1094s)
Sachs identifies security, not capability, as the defining challenge of the next six months. She references Simon Wilson's "lethal trifecta": access to private data, exposure to untrusted content (via ingestion, MCP, email, web search), and the ability to communicate externally. The more autonomous a system is, the more unsupervised this risk becomes. Sandboxes, controlled compute environments, and understanding what agents see, do, and persist are essential for building trustworthy product.

### Multi-Agent Orchestration in Notion [19:47](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-I5W5QVAT8E&t=1187s)
Sachs demonstrates how Notion orchestrates multiple agents within active documents (not markdown files). In a live example, she has Claude scope out a task, which generates a spec and asks a clarifying question. She tags in a human PM, then brings in a Decagon agent for customer-voice data, has Claude generate a PR, and asks Codex to review it — finding two issues. This multi-agent, multi-lab orchestration is the software factory vision in practice. She notes that Notion uses this internally for polish and large feedback coordination, and that Vercell does similar orchestration from staging to shipping. Customers see over three minutes saved per task.

### Closing Reflections [22:56](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-I5W5QVAT8E&t=1376s)
Sachs acknowledges the market is young, opaque, and moving fast. She expresses gratitude for communities like AI Engineer that foster open discussion, urges the audience to be critical thinkers about token economics and model strategy, and invites people to reach out via Twitter or email.
