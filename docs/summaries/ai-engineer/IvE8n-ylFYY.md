# Privacy-Preserving Intelligence — Steve Korshakov, Bee (acq. Amazon)

## TL;DR
Steve Korshakov from Bee (acquired by Amazon) explains the encryption and security architecture behind their always-on AI wearable, which captures roughly 10 million tokens per person per year. The system is built around customer-held keys, transparency logs, and confidential compute, ensuring no one at Amazon can access user data or ship unnoticed code.

## Key Takeaways
- Bee built an AI wearable microphone that records everything and builds a personal agent, capturing about 10 million tokens per user per year.
- Within a single week of recording, the device captures extremely sensitive personal data, making it one of the most privacy-sensitive capture devices on the market.
- The system is designed around a stateful, always-on runtime with persistent memory that operates autonomously without requiring the user's device to be online.
- Encryption keys are generated and stored only on the user's phone; they are never persisted server-side and cannot be disabled or bypassed.
- A sophisticated attestation pipeline verifies workload integrity against a public transparency log (using Sigstore) before keys are replicated to confidential compute nodes.
- Keys in memory have a forced seven-day expiration, chosen as a realistic useful time horizon.
- A two-tier deployment system separates the product team from a dedicated privacy/security team whose signing keys are hardcoded into client apps and backends, preventing the product team from shipping anything unnoticed.
- The entire codebase is intentionally minimal—about 20,000 lines of memory-safe code—most of which handles attestation, relying on established, trustworthy software rather than inventing custom cryptography.
- Joining Amazon changed the threat model: being inside Amazon required protecting against internal threats, not just external ones.
- For taming autonomous agents, Korshakov advocates sandboxing and limiting their ability to cause harm rather than relying on behavioral training.

## Detailed Breakdown

### Introduction and the Bee Wearable [00:01](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IvE8n-ylFYY&t=1s)
Korshakov introduces himself as being from Amazon, noting Bee was acquired about eight months prior. He presents the Bee wearable—a microphone on his wrist that records everything to build a personal AI agent. Users can extract recorded data and plug it into their own systems or agents.

### Sensitivity of Captured Data [00:33](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IvE8n-ylFYY&t=33s)
He quantifies the data: a single person captures about 10 million tokens per year. Within the first week of wearing the device, people share extremely sensitive information with friends and family, meaning virtually everything about a person can be learned in a week. He calls it one of the most sensitive capture devices on the market.

### Design Principles for the Agent [02:04](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IvE8n-ylFYY&t=124s)
Core principles include the agent working continuously and non-stop on the user's behalf without consuming customer device resources like battery. Current request-response systems are insufficient; the system needs to run continuously, sometimes for days. This led to building a stateful runtime with persistent memory that can connect to tools and third-party services while operating fully autonomously and fully controlled by the user.

### Encryption and Key Management [04:11](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IvE8n-ylFYY&t=251s)
The encryption system rests on four core ideas, with key management being the first. Keys are generated and stored only on the customer's phone (iPhone or Android); they never leave the device persistently and are not stored server-side. Encryption is mandatory with no opt-out or bypass. To protect against internal threats, all workloads are fully transparent and audited, and dependencies on trusted components are minimized.

### Attestation and Confidential Compute [05:13](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IvE8n-ylFYY&t=313s)
When the phone connects to the backend, it runs a sophisticated attestation pipeline verifying workload integrity and confirming the workload is in a public transparency log (Sigstore). Once attestation completes, the client shares the key with the main backend, which replicates it to similar nodes running within confidential compute. Unencrypted data never leaves the perimeter, and they run their own inference. Keys in memory have a forced seven-day expiration, chosen as a realistic useful time horizon—24 hours would be too short.

### Two-Tier Deployment and Code Transparency [07:18](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IvE8n-ylFYY&t=438s)
To prevent anyone inside Amazon from shipping unnoticed code, a two-tier system is used. A dedicated privacy team—separate from the product team and not controllable by them—manages the transparency log. Their signing keys are hardcoded into client apps and backends. The process is split into two parts: building a base image with measurement tools, then deploying a manifest to the transparency log. This setup enables security audit companies to trace any deployed image or data for weak spots.

### Certificates and Private CA [09:26](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IvE8n-ylFYY&t=566s)
After self-verification, the VM issues a certificate embedding all encryption proofs, transparency proofs, and attestation documents. A private CA is used because public certificates would populate a public transparency log. They plan to introduce an extra proxy for normal TLS with lighter attestation but do not have it yet.

### Q&A: Changes After Joining Amazon [10:35](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IvE8n-ylFYY&t=635s)
Korshakov explains that before joining Amazon, running on Amazon provided guarantees that Amazon couldn't see customer data. But being inside Amazon changed the threat model, requiring protection against internal threats as well. The infrastructure uses normal EC2 instances, and almost everything was built from scratch.

### Q&A: Code Minimalism and Avoiding Custom Crypto [12:09](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IvE8n-ylFYY&t=729s)
The codebase is intentionally small—about 20,000 lines in memory-safe languages—most of which verifies attestation. They reuse established, trustworthy software rather than inventing custom cryptography. Korshakov references his time at Telegram, where they reintroduced their own crypto, which he calls questionable, and says he avoids repeating that at Amazon.

### Q&A: Taming Autonomous Agents [14:05](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IvE8n-ylFYY&t=845s)
When asked about controlling autonomous agents, Korshakov says nothing works except sandboxing and not giving agents the ability to harm themselves or others. He draws an analogy to the brain not being able to stop the heart at will. He also comments on OpenAI's approach, saying that once they tightened security, the agent became much less useful, and that their approach isn't ideal. Bee's goal is to deploy a sandboxed agent that simply cannot do harmful things.
