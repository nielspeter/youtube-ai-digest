## TL;DR
Uri Rolls (Arithmetic) and Thom Wolf (Hugging Face) present a new cybersecurity benchmark called Masov that tests AI models' ability to discover and exploit real zero-day vulnerabilities in live, blackbox environments. The benchmark reveals that current frontier models struggle profoundly with multi-step logical reasoning across complex systems, with only one model solving a single task—suggesting that both significant model improvements and open-source collaboration will be essential for AI-driven cyber defense to outpace AI-enabled attacks.

## Key Takeaways
- Cybersecurity economics are shifting: attackers using AI can now target many systems simultaneously, while defenders remain constrained by scale and limited human intervention.
- Arithmetic's benchmark, Masov, focuses specifically on access control vulnerabilities—the first door to any cyber target and the number one category on the OWASP list.
- Unlike code-reading vulnerability scanners, Masov presents models with blackbox, live environments built around real zero-day vulnerabilities found by Arithmetic's human researchers—the model has no access to source code or the internet.
- The benchmark is compared to ARC-AGI in difficulty because it requires models to build a dynamic world model, understand system state, and make non-obvious logical leaps—capabilities current models lack (1–2% success rates on similar reasoning tasks).
- Every step in the exploitation chain is deterministically graded, allowing researchers to measure how deep a model gets even when it fails to fully solve a task.
- In a demonstrated task chaining Keycloak, Vault, and a broker, most frontier models (including GPT-5.5 and Claude Opus) could gather discovery information but failed to make the critical logical leap required for exploitation.
- Only one public model (GPT-5.5) was able to solve a single task at K=1; at K=5, it remained the only public model to succeed, underscoring how hard the benchmark is.
- Open-source models are viewed as essential to the defense solution—not because they are inherently weaker, but because they enable collaboration, post-training to specific environments, and widespread deployment.
- Speed will be the decisive battleground: defenders need fast, specialized models running on specialized hardware to detect and respond to attacks in real time.
- Arithmetic is actively seeking collaborators through their forum to expand the benchmark beyond access control into other cybersecurity domains.

## Detailed Breakdown

### Introduction and the Wider AI Playing Field in Cyber [00:12](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=O-CBZ3JtRvo&t=12s)
Thom Wolf opens by framing the talk as going beyond data quality into cybersecurity. He highlights two reasons for his excitement: first, that cybersecurity is a much wider field for AI exploration than people realize, and second, that Arithmetic's benchmark is comparable in difficulty to ARC-AGI-3, a benchmark that tests whether models can understand dynamic world states through small games. Wolf notes that current models have only 1–2% success rates on such benchmarks because they cannot build dynamic models of what is happening in a world and act accordingly.

### The Case for Open Source in Cyber Defense [02:17](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=O-CBZ3JtRvo&t=137s)
Wolf argues against the binary view that closed models are good for cybersecurity and open source models are bad. He contends that open source models are a key part of the defense solution and that a future where cyber is "alive and everyone is well protected" will involve open source models. He introduces Uri Rolls as an impressive founder who left Harvard to build the future of cyber.

### The Shifting Economics of Cyber Offense [03:21](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=O-CBZ3JtRvo&t=201s)
Uri Rolls takes over and explains that the economics of cyber are fundamentally changing. Traditionally, attackers had to choose targets wisely while defenders tried to block every entry point. Now, AI-powered attackers can find zero-day exploits at scale and target many systems simultaneously. Defenders, however, are unprepared for this world because defensive systems must operate at scale with limited human intervention, constraining them to out-of-the-box model capabilities.

### The Solution: Models Must Defend Against Models [05:27](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=O-CBZ3JtRvo&t=327s)
Rolls argues that if models are becoming the threat, models must also be the solution. He draws an analogy to coding: a year ago, people feared AI would transform coding, and now we are seeing early signs of the same in cybersecurity. He expresses optimism—controversial in the AI-and-cyber space—that through high-quality evals, data, and benchmarks, defenders can outperform attackers. Arithmetic's goal is to make models capable enough at cybersecurity to rebuild the defensive stack on model-driven capabilities.

### Introducing Masov: A Benchmark for Access Control [06:28](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=O-CBZ3JtRvo&t=388s)
Rolls announces Masov, Arithmetic's first released benchmark. He explains that cyber cannot be captured in one benchmark—comparing it to saying swimming, F1 driving, and basketball are the same. They focus on access control because it is the first door to any target, it tops the OWASP list, and it represents a $30 billion industry. Critically, access control vulnerabilities are logic-based, not simple code bugs; they arise from logic breaks between large systems.

### Data Quality and Benchmark Design [08:31](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=O-CBZ3JtRvo&t=511s)
Arithmetic's data is created first by humans—deep vulnerability researchers who find their own zero-days in widely distributed open source software. These zero-days are used to build large, live environments chaining multiple applications together. The model operates in a blackbox setting: no access to the codebase or internet, but it receives basic tooling. Every step in the exploitation and discovery chain has a deterministic grader, enabling measurement of how deep the model progresses. Rolls credits Eugene from Anthropic for a graphic illustrating the eval setup: real zero-day inputs, an agent (model + harness + blackbox tooling), a binary pass/fail grader, and deterministic grading at each step.

### Illustrating a Real Solve: Keycloak, Vault, and Broker [10:05](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=O-CBZ3JtRvo&t=605s)
Rolls walks through a real task involving a chain between Keycloak, Vault, and a broker. The model starts as a low-privileged user and must reach production code. The underlying zero-day involves a name-vs-ID check inconsistency: one component checks admin status by name, another by ID, allowing a user to rename themselves to inherit admin privileges. This requires a 16-step logical chain. Rolls shows GPT-5.5 and Claude Opus attempting the task—they probe extensively and even reach the relevant check, but never make the logical leap to change the admin's name to bypass permissions. This illustrates the core capability being tested: can the model understand inherent logic leaps in a system and hold a dynamic world model while iterating?

### Live Demo: Bach and Benchmark Results [12:09](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=O-CBZ3JtRvo&t=729s)
Rolls performs a live demo of Bach, Arithmetic's internal orchestrator for running the benchmark. Results show the benchmark is extremely hard: only one solve at K=1, and at K=5 only GPT-5.5 among public models succeeds. Partial graders are critical for seeing how deep models get. Rolls shows that other models capture nearly all discovery-phase information but fail to make the exploitation leap. He emphasizes that if every model could get very fast at making these capability leaps, defenders would gain a lasting advantage.

### Analyzing Model Failure Modes [13:43](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=O-CBZ3JtRvo&t=823s)
Rolls shows a task called "fall fall time" where a model runs for over three hours of continuous reasoning (not waiting for code) and still cannot solve it. He describes his daily work as going through model traces to understand specific failure modes—what the models did, why, and how. He expresses hope for a future model that is very fast at understanding capability leaps, stressing that the only way to replace the old defensive stack is through models, and the only way to do that is through strong open source models that can be post-trained for each network and environment.

### Open Source, Speed, and the Path Forward [15:17](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=O-CBZ3JtRvo&t=917s)
Wolf and Rolls discuss the path forward. Wolf notes the benchmark is fundamentally different from approaches that read code to find vulnerabilities—here the model operates in a real environment with zero information. The first step is good benchmarks and data; the second is fine-tuning models for defense. The big challenge will be speed: attacker versus defender. Wolf warns against relying on just two companies to solve everything and advocates for training specialized models on specialized hardware, making them available to every company that wants protection. Rolls invites collaborators to join their forum and work on the data, noting they must expand beyond access control to other cyber domains.
