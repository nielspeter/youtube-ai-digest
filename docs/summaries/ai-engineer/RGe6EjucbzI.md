## TL;DR
Barr Yaron presents the 2026 State of AI Engineering survey, drawing on responses from over 1,000 AI engineers, founders, and product leaders. The data reveals a field where cost has become a first-class engineering constraint, agents are rapidly gaining write access to production systems, open-weight models augment rather than replace closed ones, and teams are standardizing on platforms while keeping models flexible.

## Key Takeaways
- **1,048 respondents** participated in the survey, spanning founders, CTOs, engineers, and product people across company sizes; the field is described as "more of a discipline than a job title."
- **Text remains the dominant modality**, but audio has the highest intent-to-adopt ratio (56% of non-users plan to adopt), while image generation usage doubled from 18% to 36% year-over-year.
- **94% use closed models; 45% use open-weight models**, but over 90% of open-weight users also use closed models — open weights augment rather than replace.
- **Quality is the top model-selection criterion**, followed by agentic capabilities (tool calling) and cost; the open-vs-closed debate barely registers (only 5% name it a top-three consideration).
- **Cost is now a first-class engineering constraint**: ~75% of respondents adjust AI usage based on cost, and cost/token usage is the #2 thing monitored in production, right under quality.
- **95% of teams are using agents** (roughly double last year), and **89% of agent-using teams report their agents have write access** (up from 52%), tripling the overall share of respondents using write-enabled agents.
- **Agent guardrails remain primitive**: the top control mechanisms are human-in-the-loop approvals and gating permissions; no one has settled the control layer, and memory/persistent context is an area to watch.

## Detailed Breakdown

### Introduction and Survey Overview [00:01](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RGe6EjucbzI&t=1s)
Barr Yaron opens by noting how quickly the AI landscape shifts — Frontier model releases are now treated like national security events, and Meta is reportedly exploring selling AI compute. This volatility is exactly why the annual survey exists: to cut through the noise and capture what AI engineers are actually doing. This year's survey was run in partnership with Notion and Vercel. Yaron introduces himself as an investment partner at Amplify, investing in companies built by and for AI engineers, and promises "short time on Bar, long time on bar charts."

### Respondent Demographics [02:12](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RGe6EjucbzI&t=132s)
The survey gathered **1,048 respondents**. Yaron emphasizes that AI engineering is "more of a discipline than a job title," touching founders, CTOs, engineers, and product people across company sizes and experience levels. A consistent pattern persists for the third year: respondents skew toward senior engineers who are newer to AI. Over half of those with 10+ years of software experience have three years or less of AI experience. Meanwhile, the newest cohort of engineers has nearly as much AI experience as median 10-year software veterans — they've never known software without AI.

### Modalities: Text Dominates, Audio Has Highest Intent, Image Gen Doubles [03:45](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RGe6EjucbzI&t=225s)
Text is the dominant modality, unsurprisingly. Yaron introduces an "intent-to-adopt ratio" — among people not currently building with a modality, how many plan to? **Audio leads at 56%**, up from 37% last year, showing accelerating interest. However, the biggest year-over-year jump is in **image generation**, which doubled from 18% to 36% of respondents using it and feeling good about it. Yaron attributes this to product improvements (Nano Banana, Nano Banana 2, ChatGPT images 2.0) crossing a quality threshold. Audio has the strongest intent; image generation shows what happens when a modality crosses it.

### Model Usage: Open Weights Augment, Quality Drives Choice [05:50](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RGe6EjucbzI&t=350s)
**94% use closed models; 45% use open-weight models.** But over 90% of open-weight users also use closed models, making open weights an augmentation rather than a replacement. When choosing models, the open-vs-closed debate is a top-three consideration for only 5% of respondents. What actually matters: **quality dominates**, followed by agentic capabilities (tool calling) and cost tied together. Reliability was named by only one in five — Yaron's interpretation is that it has become a threshold requirement; models are reliable enough that the decision moves up the stack to quality, capability, and cost.

### Multi-Model Routing and Tool Standardization [07:21](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RGe6EjucbzI&t=441s)
**87% of teams use more than one model** — the opposite of standardization. Teams route by task type, compare outputs across models, or route based on cost. However, more than half of respondents say their organizations are starting to standardize on fewer AI tools, trading flexibility for standardization. The headline: **standardization is happening at the platform and tools layer, not the models layer.**

### Cost as a First-Class Engineering Constraint [08:25](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RGe6EjucbzI&t=505s)
Yaron calls this the slide where anyone who's opened an AI bill starts nodding. **40% say cost regularly shapes how ambitiously they use AI**, and another 36% say it sometimes does — roughly three out of four respondents adjust AI usage based on cost. This is also reflected in production monitoring, where **cost and token usage is the #2 monitored metric**, right under quality, effectively treated like an SLA. Yaron notes this would have been surprising 12 months ago.

### Agents: Escaping Demo World with Write Access [09:56](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RGe6EjucbzI&t=596s)
**95% of respondents say they're using agents**, roughly double last year. More strikingly, among teams using agents, **89% report their agents have write access**, up from 52% last year. Combined, the share of all respondents using write-enabled agents has more than tripled. Agents are no longer just reading, summarizing, and drafting — they're taking actions inside systems.

### Agent Control and Failure Modes [11:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RGe6EjucbzI&t=660s)
Control mechanisms for agents remain fairly blunt. The top two are **human-in-the-loop approvals and gating permissions** — which Yaron compares to "the same toolkit you'd use to manage an intern." Below that, approaches scatter: task decomposition, retrieval, memory, sandboxing. Nobody has settled the control layer. Yaron highlights **memory and persistent context** as an area to watch for significant evolution. When agents fail, the problem is usually the thinking, not the plumbing: **two-thirds say hallucination or losing context mid-task is what frustrates them most.**

### Stack Challenges: Evals Still #1, Build vs. Buy [12:01](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RGe6EjucbzI&t=721s)
**Evals remain the #1 stack challenge** for the third year running, though the margin is thinning. Yaron notes that 96% of respondents have a problem with the stack — they just can't agree on which one, and that scatter is the map for builders deciding what to build next. The "vibe review" remains the most common evaluation method. On build vs. buy across eight stack layers: **inference and model serving is the most-bought layer**, while **prompt management is the most-built (61% build in-house)**. Product logic, prompts, RAG, and eval tend to stay in-house. **Fine-tuning is the clearest "not yet"** — most don't do it at all, and those who bought aren't looking to build (and vice versa).

### Impact on Teams and Roles [14:07](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RGe6EjucbzI&t=847s)
**97% report a net positive effect** on their organization. The top effect isn't just speed — it's cheaper failure, more experimentation, more prototypes, more bets. AI made trying things nearly free. But it's not free-free: the same tools increase review burden, and **over 90% report negative downstream effects**, most commonly erosion of deep technical skills and codebase understanding. **81% say AI is blurring the line between engineering and product/design/marketing roles.** Over a third of teams have non-developers shipping features (mostly smaller/internal), and **17% say non-developers regularly ship customer-facing features**. Even when non-developers aren't shipping, a third of teams see them building useful prototypes and front-end mocks.

### Bets: Present and Five-Year [16:43](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RGe6EjucbzI&t=1003s)
**76% say AI boosted their job satisfaction.** However, **59% fear today's AI code creates long-term liabilities**, and only a third call software engineering a "solved problem." For five-year bets: **67% expect a leading lab will declare AGI** (Yaron notes the wording — they asked about the press release, not the achievement). Only **9% bet on Transformers being state-of-the-art in five years**; most are unsure. The most divisive question: will there be more AI compute in space or on land? **36% yes, 38% no** — the closest split in the entire survey.

### 2026 Wrapped and Closing [18:17](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RGe6EjucbzI&t=1097s)
Yaron recaps the year: impact is overwhelmingly positive; image generation doubled while audio maintains the highest adoption intent; cost became a first-class constraint visible in monitoring and product decisions; open weights augment but don't replace, pointing to a multi-model future with stack consolidation; agents gained write access more than ever, tripling year-over-year, while guardrails stayed primitive; inference is a buy market, and everything closer to product logic tends to stay in-house. The full report is available online, and Yaron invites suggestions for the 2027 survey.
