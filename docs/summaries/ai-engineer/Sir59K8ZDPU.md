## TL;DR
Frank Coyle argues that agentic AI systems need ontologies—formal knowledge representations—to keep probabilistic LLMs on guardrails. By combining neural networks with symbolic AI (neuro-symbolic AI), developers can use graph-based ontologies and reasoning tools like RDFS and OWL to validate agent outputs, catch errors, and prevent drift in agentic loops.

## Key Takeaways
- **Neuro-symbolic AI** combines probabilistic LLMs with formal symbolic representations (ontologies, knowledge graphs) to keep agents grounded and on track.
- **Ontologies** are formal specifications of shared conceptualizations—representations of entities, their properties, and their relationships—rooted in philosophy dating back to Aristotle.
- **Graph databases** offer flexibility over relational databases, allowing you to attach new properties and relationships without restructuring tables.
- Ontologies can be built **top-down** (experts defining entities and relationships) or **bottom-up** (discovering entities and relationships from real-world data like customer interactions).
- Existing taxonomies like **schema.org**, **FOAF**, **Dublin Core**, and **DBpedia** should be leveraged rather than reinventing the wheel.
- **Loops** give agents Turing completeness (sequence, conditionals, iteration), but they introduce risks: infinite loops, drift between agents, and escalating token costs.
- **LLMs can't execute actions**—they predict the next word and formulate tool calls, but the system must execute those calls and validate results.
- **Pydantic** provides type checking at the door; ontologies provide semantic validation at the ledger—agents should ideally have no side effects until validated.
- **RDFS and OWL** enable inference and constraint checking: domain/range rules, transitive properties, functional properties, and disjoint properties catch errors that are difficult to catch in plain text.
- The overarching philosophy: "Nothing is a mistake, there's no win, no fail, only make"—get hands-on and build to learn.

### Two Lineages: Agents and Ontologies [02:22](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Sir59K8ZDPU&t=142s)
Coyle traces two intellectual lineages. **Agents** date to early AI—John McCarthy, Selfridge, Marvin Minsky's *Society of Mind*—and the 1956 Dartmouth workshop that coined "artificial intelligence." The agent concept evolved into entities that perceive, decide, and act. **Ontologies** are older, originating with Aristotle's categories of being, later formalized by philosopher W.V.O. Quine and, in 1993, by Tom Gruber, who defined an ontology as "a formal specification of a shared conceptualization." Coyle frames this as what we want to give agents: our conceptualization of our domains.

### Neuro-Symbolic AI and Convergence [04:08](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Sir59K8ZDPU&t=248s)
We're seeing the convergence of probabilistic systems (LLMs/agents) with formal representations (ontologies/knowledge graphs). This convergence is called **neuro-symbolic AI**—neural networks tied to symbolic AI (rule-based systems, knowledge graphs). Coyle argues neuro-symbolic AI keeps LLMs on guardrails. He reframes hallucination not as a bug but as a feature: LLMs imagine things that may not exist, much like humans do, and sometimes turn those imaginings into reality.

### What Ontologies Are and Graph Databases [05:09](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Sir59K8ZDPU&t=309s)
Ontologies represent entities, their relationships, and their properties. Graph databases arose because relational databases were too restrictive—adding new data required adding columns and restructuring tables. Graph databases let you simply attach new properties or relationships to existing nodes. Coyle notes this flexibility is central to why ontologies and graph representations are powerful for modeling domains.

### Building Ontologies: Top-Down and Bottom-Up [06:13](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Sir59K8ZDPU&t=373s)
Two approaches exist. **Top-down**: experts analyze a domain, identify entities (purchase orders, customers, customer reps), define their properties and relationships. This mirrors 1980s expert systems work—symbolic AI that attracted massive investment (including Japan's Fifth Generation Project) but couldn't scale, leading to AI winter. **Bottom-up**: discover entities and relationships from real-world data (e.g., customer interactions) and add them to the ontology. Coyle also highlights existing taxonomies to leverage: **schema.org**, **FOAF** (Friend of a Friend for social networks), **Dublin Core** (for research papers/books), and **DBpedia** (which underlies Wikipedia's search).

### Augmenting Technologies: RDFS and OWL [09:24](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Sir59K8ZDPU&t=564s)
Beyond the graph itself, auxiliary technologies add inference and constraint capabilities. **RDFS** provides domain and range rules: if "teaches" has a domain of "teacher" and a range of "student," then from "Bob teaches Scooter" you can infer Bob is a teacher (and a person, if all teachers are persons) and Scooter is a student. **OWL** adds further reasoning: **transitive properties** (if Sue is an ancestor of Mary and Mary of Ann, then Sue is an ancestor of Ann), **functional properties** (has father—only one, so "Bob is Jim's father" and "BB is Jim's father" implies Bob and BB are the same individual), and **disjoint properties** (customer and support rep are separate, non-overlapping entities). These rules sit alongside the graph and augment it with derived data and constraints.

### Agents, Loops, and Turing Completeness [11:59](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Sir59K8ZDPU&t=719s)
Everyone's talking about agents and loops. Coyle connects this to Bohm and Jacopini's 1966 result: any programming language with sequence, conditionals, and loops is **Turing complete**—capable of computing anything computable. Agents with loops now have all three pieces, making agentic AI computationally universal. But loops carry dangers: they can break (infinite loops), drift (agents talking to each other go off track), and cost money (token counts climb as loops iterate). Coyle sees this as revisiting symbolic AI and expert systems.

### Claude Agent Code Walkthrough [14:24](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Sir59K8ZDPU&t=864s)
Coyle walks through a Python example using a Claude agent. The code has a `while True` loop. The first chunk sends a model, a prompt, and a tool to the LLM, asking it to solve a problem using the tool. The LLM can't execute anything—it only predicts the next word—but it formulates the tool call parameters and returns them. The second chunk checks the **stop reason**: if it's "tool use," the system extracts and executes the tool call. After the tool runs, Coyle highlights (in red) where ontologies come in: the tool's output is fed to a validator that uses domain ontologies to assess whether the LLM's response is reasonable. If reasonable, proceed; if not, loop back to the LLM or bring in a human. The key idea: surround the input with checks.

### Pydantic, Side Effects, and Validation [17:48](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Sir59K8ZDPU&t=1068s)
Coyle recommends **Pydantic** for type-checking parameters—since Python is untyped, Pydantic adds structure at the door. The ontology then serves as the semantic validator at the ledger. He advises that agents should have **no side effects** until validated—don't let them modify the database before the ontology check passes.

### OWL Error-Catching Examples [18:48](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Sir59K8ZDPU&t=1128s)
Coyle shows specific logical constructs from OWL and the errors they catch: a second refund on the same order (caught by functional properties), a payout sent to support desk instead of buyer (caught by disjoint properties between customer and support rep), and a made-up status like "probably shipped" (caught by constraining values to paid, shipped, or refunded—nothing else). These are hard to catch in plain English text but straightforward with ontology-based reasoning.

### Closing and Contact [20:21](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Sir59K8ZDPU&t=1221s)
Coyle reiterates his bottom line: use a reasoner built on ontology to keep the LLM on track, with guardrails from RDFS and OWL. He closes with his philosophy—"nothing is a mistake, there's no win, no fail, only make"—and shares his contact (coyle@berkeley) and website, codesupreme.ai, named after John Coltrane's *A Love Supreme*.
