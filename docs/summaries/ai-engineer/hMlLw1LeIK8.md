## TL;DR
Chintan Agrawal and Daniel Wirjo from AWS explore the critical but overlooked problem of turn-taking in voice agents—how systems detect when a user has finished speaking and when to respond. They present three progressive approaches (Silero VAD, STT-based turn detection, and a local Smart Turn model) and break down the latency budget of voice pipelines, emphasizing that even with ~755 ms response times, intelligent turn detection can make the experience feel far more natural.

## Key Takeaways
- **The 200 ms constraint**: Humans switch turns in conversation in about 200 ms; at 800 ms things feel off, and at 1.5 s users hang up. Voice agents don't get the luxury of text-chat latency.
- **Turn-taking is an audio engineering problem, not an LLM problem**: The same model and prompt can produce vastly different user experiences depending solely on the audio pipeline's turn-taking behavior.
- **VAD is the tiny but critical component**: Voice Activity Detection (VAD) sits at the front of the pipeline and controls turn-taking by detecting whether the user has stopped talking.
- **Three levels of turn detection**: Level 1 is local Silero VAD (silence detection), Level 2 delegates to STT providers (Cartesia, Deepgram), and Level 3 combines VAD with a local Smart Turn model that uses prosody and intonation.
- **Silero VAD limitations**: A 2 MB, 300K-parameter model that detects speech but cannot distinguish between a completed sentence, a thinking pause, a breath, or a backchannel acknowledgement—all silences look the same.
- **STT-based turn detection trades transparency for intelligence**: Providers like Cartesia and Deepgram use full audio signals plus linguistic context, but when they misfire, you have no visibility into why.
- **Smart Turn V3.2**: An 8 MB, BSD-2-licensed model with 58.9% recall and 68.4% precision that runs during silence; when it's not confident, the VAD timer acts as a safety net.
- **Latency budget breakdown**: In a standard cloud API setup, total voice-to-voice latency is ~1,100–1,300 ms, with STT + LLM consuming roughly two-thirds of that budget.
- **P95 tail latency matters more than P50 in voice**: GPT-4.1 performs well at P50 (~536 ms) but spikes to 1.7 s at P95; Claude 3 hit over 4 seconds—one slow response breaks the entire conversational flow.
- **False interruptions increase escalation rates**: When agents cut people off incorrectly, users are more likely to request human support, making turn detection accuracy a business-critical concern.

## Detailed Breakdown

### The Problem: Turn-Taking in Voice Agents [00:03](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hMlLw1LeIK8&t=3s)
Chintan introduces the core topic: how does a voice agent know when a user has finished speaking and it's time to respond? He emphasizes this is an audio engineering problem, not an LLM problem—you can have the perfect model and transcript, but the experience still feels broken if turn-taking is off. He and Daniel will walk through three approaches, with Daniel running live demos.

### The 200 Millisecond Constraint [01:06](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hMlLw1LeIK8&t=66s)
Humans switch turns in conversation in about 200 ms. At 800 ms, things start to feel off; at 1.5 s, users hang up. Unlike text-based HR chat agents that might tolerate 5-second responses, voice agents have no such luxury. Chintan references Salesforce's published results (March 26) showing a best measured response time of 755 ms—still nearly 4x slower than natural human turn-taking. The question becomes how to close this gap, or at least get turn-taking right so the experience feels better than raw numbers suggest.

### Demonstrating the Problem [01:37](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hMlLw1LeIK8&t=97s)
Chintan shows two side-by-side examples with the same user, same sentence, and same LLM. On the left, when a user tries to correct themselves mid-sentence ("I want to fly..."), the agent doesn't notice and keeps going for almost 2 seconds. On the right, the agent captures the interruption in under 200 ms and immediately backs off. The difference is purely the audio pipeline—how fast it detects someone talking and knows when to stop.

### Pipeline Architecture and Where Turn-Taking Lives [03:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hMlLw1LeIK8&t=220s)
The standard pipeline includes STT, LLM, and TTS (audio in, text out, text in, audio out). The often-missed piece is Voice Activity Detection (VAD)—a tiny component at the front that detects whether the user has stopped talking. Two additional components work alongside it: **Smart Turn detection**, which watches the VAD signal plus audio features to decide whether to respond or wait, and the **Interruption Handler**, which triggers when someone barges in—flushing the TTS and canceling LLM generation so the pipeline is ready for new input within ~50 ms.

### Level 1: Silero VAD [05:13](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hMlLw1LeIK8&t=313s)
Silero VAD is a small 300K-parameter, 2 MB model that takes raw audio, converts it to spectral features via short-term Fourier transform, uses four convolutional layers and an LSTM for cross-frame memory, and outputs a sigmoid probability of speech. The key parameter is **minimum silence milliseconds**—set it too low and the agent snaps and cuts people off; too high and you get dead air. A sales agent might use 200 ms; a domain needing more response time might use 1,000–1,200 ms. VAD works well for basic speech detection but cannot distinguish between a completed sentence, a thinking pause, a breath, or a backchannel acknowledgement—all silences look identical to it.

### Level 2: STT-Based Turn Detection [08:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hMlLw1LeIK8&t=500s)
At Level 2, the STT service handles turn detection. Cartesia does turn detection inside its STT websocket, emitting events when it thinks a turn is done; Deepgram does the same with endpointing. P50 latencies are ~300 ms for Cartesia and ~250 ms for Deepgram Nova 3. These use full audio signals plus linguistic context—far more information than VAD alone. The trade-off is transparency: when it works, it's great, but when it misfires, there's no log explaining why, and the decision was made on someone else's server.

### Level 3: VAD + Smart Turn [09:55](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hMlLw1LeIK8&t=595s)
Level 3 keeps Silero VAD running locally for basic speech detection and adds Smart Turn V3.2 on top—a small model that runs during silence. Smart Turn achieves 58.9% recall and 68.4% precision, meaning it catches about 6 out of 10 completed sentences quickly. When it's not confident, the VAD timer kicks in as a safety net (e.g., at 300 ms), so you're never stuck waiting. Chintan references a Meta paper from March reporting 87.7% recall, but the code wasn't released. Smart Turn is BSD-2-licensed, 8 MB, and pip-installable. Crucially, Level 3 also enables intelligent interruption classification—distinguishing a real correction (stop everything), a filler/backchannel (keep going, maybe quieter), or a cough/background noise (ignore and finish).

### Code Comparison Across Three Levels [13:02](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hMlLw1LeIK8&t=782s)
Chintan shows that the pipeline code is nearly identical across all three levels—only the configuration differs. In Level 1, you pass a Silero VAD analyzer (user silent for 300 ms = done). In Level 2, you swap in a Cartesia STT service class that tells you when the turn is over. In Level 3, you keep Silero VAD but add a local Smart Turn analyzer that examines prosody and intonation to decide whether a pause means "I'm done" or "I'm still thinking."

### Latency Budget Breakdown [14:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hMlLw1LeIK8&t=880s)
Using production measurements from Quintela (co-founder of Daily.co, creator of Pipe Cat), Chintan breaks down where time goes: audio capture ~40 ms, network and jitter buffer ~52 ms, transcription plus endpointing ~300 ms, LLM time to first byte ~500–650 ms (the dominant bottleneck), and network out plus TTS playback ~85–120 ms. Total: ~1,100–1,300 ms for standard cloud API setups. The Pipe Cat team demonstrated ~500 ms by co-locating all models on the same GPU cluster, eliminating network hops—an achievable floor with infrastructure investment. STT and LLM together consume roughly two-thirds of the latency budget, making them the only meaningful levers.

### LLM Benchmarking for Voice [17:19](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hMlLw1LeIK8&t=1039s)
Chintan benchmarks LLMs specifically for voice, targeting under 700 ms time to first token. As of June 2026: Nemotron-3 Ultra achieves 529 ms P50, GPT-4.1 at 536 ms P50. But P95 tail latency matters more in voice—GPT-4.1 spikes to 1.7 s at P95, and Claude 3 hit over 4 seconds. In conversation, you can't average this out; one slow response breaks the flow. He also highlights multi-turn drift: after 15–20 turns, models may ignore system prompt parts, become verbose, or go off-script—requiring context pruning or session resets.

### Production Issues and Recommendations [20:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hMlLw1LeIK8&t=1200s)
False interruptions significantly increase escalation rates—when agents cut people off incorrectly, users request human support. At 755 ms best-case voice-to-voice latency, we're nowhere near human speed, so turn-taking must do heavy lifting to make the experience feel better than raw numbers suggest. Smart Turn's 58.9% recall is the best deployable option today, though 4 out of 10 turns still fall back to the VAD timer. Running five systems that scale and fail differently is hard; Chintan suggests Picovoice Cloud for managed hosting or AWS guidance reference architecture for enterprise deployments.

Daniel then unmutes and says "Sydney," completing the sentence, and the agent responds appropriately. ### Demo 3: Smart Turn Model [29:30](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hMlLw1LeIK8&t=1770s)
The third example runs the Smart Turn model locally alongside Silero VAD. When Daniel pauses after "I'm thinking of going to um," the end-of-turn state is classified as incomplete, but the turn eventually completes due to the silence fallback (VAD timer). This demonstrates the model's ability to distinguish genuine completion from a pause.
