## TL;DR
Dr. Sajjan Kanukolanu explains why bolting AI onto legacy GTM stacks fails and presents a three-layer agent architecture—signals, buyer intelligence, and action—that identifies anonymous visitors, builds a context graph, and triggers personalized outreach. He demonstrates Position Squared's system in practice and outlines common failure points (ICP drift, alert fatigue, identity ceiling, human bottleneck) along with four key takeaways for sustainable AI-driven GTM.

## Key Takeaways
- By the time B2B buyers reach a vendor, 80% have already shortlisted vendors and only 17% of buying time is spent talking to potential vendors.
- Bolting AI onto existing GTM systems doesn't scale because AI alone can't identify buyers, legacy stacks can't capture intent signals, and the underlying architecture must evolve with AI at the core.
- A three-layer architecture—signals, buyer intelligence, and action—must run in tandem for a GTM system to work in the AI era.
- A context graph unifies persona, account, and deal-level signals into a connected record per buyer, enabling prioritization of high-intent accounts.
- Social signals (especially LinkedIn job changes) are critical but underutilized; a champion leaving one company becomes a new opportunity at their next company.
- ICPs drift over time; agents must be retrained quarterly using closed-won and closed-lost data to avoid acting on stale criteria.
- Alert fatigue kills system trust—use the context graph to surface only the alerts that matter to sales reps.
- Identity ceiling is a structural limit: de-anonymization tools are ~70% accurate at company level but only 15–20% at the individual level.
- If an AI-drafted email takes longer than 30 seconds to edit, sales reps will abandon the system and write their own.
- Every send, reply, and closed deal should feed back into the knowledge base so the flywheel compounds over time.

### Three Problems with Bolting AI onto Legacy GTM Stacks [01:35](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ltv-L5oMPIs&t=95s)
Many GTM teams try to bolt AI onto existing stacks, which hinders scale. Three problems must be solved simultaneously: (1) AI alone can't identify the buyer, their role, history, or intent; (2) legacy GTM stacks can't capture and consolidate the intent signals and CRM context AI generates; (3) the underlying architecture must evolve with AI at the core. Solving only one or two is insufficient—all three must be addressed together.

### Buyer Behavior Statistics [03:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ltv-L5oMPIs&t=220s)
Citing Forrester 2026 research, he shares that 94% of buyers use GenAI as a primary research tool, 67% prefer a rep-free experience, 80% of deals go to buyers on the pre-contact list, and only 17% of total buying time is spent talking with potential vendors. This means buyers arrive late-stage and have largely made up their minds. Generic chat greetings like "How can I help you?" actually move the conversation backward.

### Layer One: Signals [06:19](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ltv-L5oMPIs&t=379s)
The first architecture layer captures signals from CRM systems (deals, contacts, sales rep ownership), enrichment systems that de-anonymize anonymous visitors, and social media—especially LinkedIn. Job changes are highlighted as a powerful signal: when an executive sponsor leaves one company for another, that new company should automatically become a target account with the departing contact as a new champion.

### Layer Two: Buyer Intelligence [08:23](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ltv-L5oMPIs&t=503s)
The second layer centers on a knowledge base containing product context, ICPs, personas, playbooks, success criteria, de-anonymized visitor identity information, and ICP scoring definitions. A context builder assembles signals into a context graph, and routing logic determines what message goes to whom, when, and through which channel (LinkedIn, email, ads).

### Layer Three: Action [09:58](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ltv-L5oMPIs&t=598s)
The action layer personalizes the chat experience—addressing users by name and referencing prior engagement rather than asking generic questions. It also includes sending the right volume of alerts to sales reps (too many causes disengagement), updating the CRM with account context (not just contact logs), and triggering the appropriate outreach sequences based on signals.

### The Context Graph [11:33](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ltv-L5oMPIs&t=693s)
All signals roll into a connected record per buyer. Persona, account, and deal-level data are logged for every touchpoint. A person's signals link to their account; accounts contain multiple people, so buying committee information surfaces per account. Deal-level information tracks whether sales and marketing have already reached out. Without this context graph, there's no way to prioritize high-intent accounts.

### Implementation: Connected Agents in Practice [13:08](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ltv-L5oMPIs&t=788s)
The architecture is implemented as connected agents. When an anonymous visitor lands on the site, an identification job kicks off using multiple de-anonymization sources (no single tool catches every visitor). Results are consolidated and deduplicated, then sent to an enrichment phase. An ICP filter agent checks contacts against knowledge base criteria (industry, geo, size, persona), weeding out non-fits. A match agent checks contacts against the CRM to determine if they're warm or hot. Finally, an action agent decides next steps per person or buying committee—Slack alerts with context, draft emails, email sends, or LinkedIn outreach.

### Demo: Personalized Chat and Dashboard [16:44](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ltv-L5oMPIs&t=1004s)
He demonstrates Position Squared's "Intelligence" system. The chat interface recognizes him on landing and references a prior conversation rather than asking generic questions. The internal dashboard shows two sections: GTM and organic. The anonymous visitors section identifies ~3,000 visitors from ~280 accounts, categorizing by industry (IT is a key vertical) and surfacing hot signals combined with HubSpot conversation data. The LinkedIn intelligence section shows ~100 visitors from 73 companies engaging with eight recent posts, sortable by seniority (C-suite, VPs, directors, managers, ICs) and company.

### What Breaks: Four Key Failure Points [20:55](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ltv-L5oMPIs&t=1255s)

**ICP Drift [21:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ltv-L5oMPIs&t=1260s)** — ICPs evolve; if you close a deal in a new vertical or ARR range, that must be reflected in the knowledge base. Agents should be retrained quarterly with closed-won and closed-lost data.

**Alert Fatigue [22:15](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ltv-L5oMPIs&t=1335s)** — If everything is flagged as hot, reps stop acting and stop trusting the system. Use the knowledge base and context graph to cut through noise and surface only meaningful alerts.

**Identity Ceiling [22:45](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ltv-L5oMPIs&t=1365s)** — De-anonymization tools are ~70% accurate at company identification but only 15–20% at the individual level. This is a structural limit; the only mitigation is continuously testing different tools and keeping these accuracy metrics in mind when planning outreach.

**Human Bottleneck [23:30](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ltv-L5oMPIs&t=1410s)** — If the approval queue feels like a chore, it becomes backlogged and ignored. If editing an AI-drafted email takes longer than 30 seconds, reps will write their own. The system must shrink friction to a one-click send for near-ready drafts.

### Four Key Takeaways [24:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ltv-L5oMPIs&t=1480s)
1. **Start with identity** — Not being able to identify visitors is a dead GTM project; build a robust identification system with a feedback loop back into the knowledge base.
2. **Score fit and intent separately** — Conflating them sends the wrong message to the wrong person.
3. **Build an auditable, adjustable policy engine** — GTM leaders must be able to identify and fix broken agents or workflow steps without relying on developers.
4. **Let the flywheel compound** — Every send, reply, win, loss, and deferral should feed back into the knowledge base, making the system smarter over time.
