## TL;DR
AI agents often produce semantically different outputs for the same input, not merely due to the stochastic nature of LLMs, but because the inputs sit on ambiguous decision boundaries. Diane Lin explains how to use active learning to identify these "gray zone" data points and resolve the inconsistencies by augmenting agents with semantic and episodic memory, rather than immediately resorting to expensive fine-tuning.

## Key Takeaways
- Inconsistency in AI agents isn't just about wording; agents can produce semantically opposite verdicts for the exact same input across different runs, eroding user trust.
- Data points that "flip-flop" in their outputs are typically concentrated around the decision boundary—the "gray zone" where even human experts disagree.
- Gray zone ambiguity usually stems from missing context, such as specific customer policies or preferences, meaning the inconsistency is not inherently the model's fault.
- Active learning, specifically a "query by committee" approach that identifies disagreements across multiple runs, is an effective way to surface problematic data points.
- LLM uncertainty scores are unreliable for identifying errors; looking for disagreements across multiple runs or different models provides a much stronger signal.
- Fine-tuning is an expensive and heavy solution to fix inconsistencies; a lighter-weight alternative is augmenting the agent with memory.
- Semantic memory involves injecting explicit domain knowledge and rules to sharpen the decision boundary.
- Episodic memory involves automatically referencing past similar cases and their historical decisions to resolve recurring situations without human intervention.
- In an experiment with 93 cybersecurity alerts, applying episodic memory reduced the inconsistency rate from 25% to 10%, with the remaining cases routed to human review.
- Model disagreement should be treated as a feature, not a bug—each disagreement is a valuable opportunity for the model to learn and adapt to the customer's environment.

## Detailed Breakdown

### Speaker Background and the Inconsistency Problem [00:02](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wEc9aG7cRQc&t=2s)
Diane Lin introduces herself and her background, which spans AGI research (continual learning at Imperial College London, one-shot learning at MIT, and zero-shot transfer learning at Vicarious, now part of Google DeepMind) to applied machine learning in cybersecurity at Zscaler. She co-founded a company building AI agents to auto-triage security alerts, which was acquired by Datadog, where she now leads the development of self-evolved agents. The core problem she addresses is AI agent inconsistency: when the same model produces semantically different outputs for the exact same input. She clarifies that this goes beyond the stochastic nature of LLMs and poses a significant trust issue for users.

### Real-World Examples of Agent Inconsistency [03:09](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wEc9aG7cRQc&t=189s)
Lin illustrates the inconsistency problem using two different domains. In a standard NLP sentiment analysis task (labeling hotel reviews positive or negative), an LLM might occasionally flip-flop its verdict on boundary-case reviews. In cybersecurity, triaging an alert about a failed login attempt from a suspicious IP can result in the agent labeling it benign in one run and suspicious in another. This flip-flopping causes a major trust issue for customers and security analysts, who won't know which verdict to trust—especially in competitive POC bake-offs where consistent vendors will win deals.

### The Root Cause: The Decision Boundary "Gray Zone" [06:21](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wEc9aG7cRQc&t=381s)
The data points that tend to flip-flop are concentrated around the decision boundary, what Lin calls the "gray zone." Using the hotel review example, a review that is slightly positive but has negative elements sits on the boundary; even human experts would disagree on how to label it, as the correct answer depends on a specific hotel's policy. Similarly, in cybersecurity, repeated failed login attempts (an attacker knocking on the door but not getting in) might be ignored by one enterprise but flagged by another. The inconsistency is not the AI agent's fault; the agent is simply pointing out existing ambiguity that requires additional information or company preference to disambiguate.

### Identifying the Gray Zone via Active Learning [11:05](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wEc9aG7cRQc&t=665s)
To fix the inconsistency, one must first identify where it occurs. Lin points to active learning, a traditional machine learning technique used to select data points where the model is most likely to make a mistake. In traditional ML, this is done by looking at uncertainty scores (probabilities close to 0.5) or using "query by committee" (running multiple models and identifying disagreements). Human reviewers then label these high-information data points to retrain the model. Lin notes that the LLM approach is similar, but requires refinement in selection strategy and retraining.

### Selection Strategy: Disagreement Over Uncertainty [14:15](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wEc9aG7cRQc&t=855s)
When adapting active learning for LLMs and AI agents, Lin advises against relying on LLM uncertainty scores, as "the LM model doesn't know what it doesn't know"—high confidence does not guarantee correctness. Instead, she recommends using the disagreement signal (query by committee) by running the same model multiple times or using different models to find where verdicts diverge. This disagreement reliably indicates where the agent needs human guidance and clarification.

### A Lightweight Solution: Semantic and Episodic Memory [15:47](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wEc9aG7cRQc&t=947s)
Instead of immediately resorting to expensive fine-tuning to resolve inconsistencies, Lin proposes a lighter-weight solution: augmenting the agent with two types of memory. Semantic memory involves explicitly adding domain knowledge and rules to the knowledge base (e.g., "if a customer complains about something outside the hotel's control, classify as positive" or "password spray without successful login is benign"). This sharpens the decision boundary. Episodic memory, on the other hand, involves automatically referencing past similar cases and their historical labels to make a decision without waiting for a human to distill the reasoning into rules.

### Combining Semantic and Episodic Memory [20:03](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wEc9aG7cRQc&t=1203s)
Lin explains that semantic and episodic memory are complementary. Episodic memory is used to automatically resolve recurring situations (such as recurring false-positive security alerts), requiring less immediate human intervention. For new cases that have no past reference or still disagree after referencing past cases, human review is required. The human then distills the domain knowledge and feeds it into semantic memory for future use. This approach yields three benefits: significantly improved consistency, highly efficient quality control (reviewing only a subset of problematic outputs), and a model that adapts to the customer's specific environment.

### Experimental Results and Final Takeaways [22:10](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wEc9aG7cRQc&t=1330s)
Lin shares experimental results from 93 real cybersecurity alerts. Without the proposed solution, 25% of the alerts flip-flopped their verdict across runs. After applying episodic memory, 15% became consistent automatically. The remaining 10% that still flipped were routed to human review, providing additional knowledge to disambiguate the tricky cases and adapt to the customer environment. Lin concludes with three takeaways: inconsistency is usually a labeling/information issue, not a model problem; model disagreement is a feature and an opportunity to learn; and fine-tuning isn't the only option—augmenting agents with semantic and episodic memory is highly effective.
