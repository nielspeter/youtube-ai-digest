# What the Best Engineers Do When Agents Fail

[![What the Best Engineers Do When Agents Fail](https://i.ytimg.com/vi/M1i11zsqsT8/hqdefault.jpg)](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=M1i11zsqsT8)

## TL;DR
Kitze, a solo builder managing 140 projects, explains how experienced engineers can dramatically outperform others by building structured "agent setups"—custom rules, self-correcting loops, and persistent skills—rather than simply prompting models and hoping for the best. The conversation covers the shift from vibe coding to "vibe engineering," the importance of refining your workflow over chasing new models, and the blunt reality that engineers who refuse to adapt will be replaced by those who can orchestrate agents effectively.

## Key Takeaways
- **Vibe coding vs. vibe engineering:** Vibe coding is trial-and-error prompting; vibe engineering is experienced developers driving agents with structured rules, skills, and guardrails to get dramatically better results.
- **The "police script" concept:** Kitze maintains custom rule files that catch issues linters and TypeScript can't, and every agent turn ends with a self-correcting loop that runs TypeScript, linter, formatter, and the police check—aiming for zero warnings every time.
- **Capture behavior, don't just prompt:** When something goes wrong, distill the desired behavior into static code analysis, hooks, agent files, or skill files. Most people keep prompting instead of codifying their knowledge.
- **Stop chasing models, build depth:** Switching providers and models every week resets your accumulated practices. Engineers who go deep with one provider and one model often outship those who constantly jump to the "smarter" model.
- **Humans are the bottleneck:** Current agent orchestrator UIs (sidebar + chat) don't scale to managing many parallel projects. The cognitive load of reviewing agent work is the real limiting factor.
- **The comfy cushion is gone:** Engineers who coasted on past skills without curiosity or willingness to adapt will face a rude awakening. The bar has risen—mid-level engineers with agent orchestration skills can outperform stubborn senior teams.
- **Cost is justified:** Paying $30–40/hour for world-class agent intelligence paired with good loops and processes is a bargain compared to human consulting rates.
- **Ego is the enemy of progress:** The best engineers unblock themselves and others, setting aside personal opinions for the greater goal. Stubborn team members become bottlenecks.
- **Agents will flip the org chart:** Kitze envisions agents crawling from peer-programming helpers to team managers and beyond, with humans eventually serving primarily as unblockers at lower levels.
- **Start with a reference codebase:** Never start fresh with nothing—always point the agent at an existing codebase or template so it doesn't choose its own stack and go off track.

## Detailed Breakdown

### Vibe Coding vs. Vibe Engineering [00:32](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=M1i11zsqsT8&t=32s)
Kitze distinguishes between vibe coding—people with little technical knowledge throwing prompts at the wall hoping something sticks—and vibe engineering, where experienced engineers drive agents with clear direction and get 20× better results. He pushes back on the dismissive meme around vibe coding, noting that most people's pre-LLM personal websites and apps were also terrible, and the industry is suddenly on a "high horse" judging AI-generated gradients that most developers couldn't write three years ago.

### Frustration, Swearing, and Model Choice [02:05](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=M1i11zsqsT8&t=125s)
Kitze admits to yelling at models when they underperform, describing the familiar arc of being impressed for 15 minutes before frustration sets in. He finds GPT-5.5 in the Codex app to be the smoothest, most stable agent—comparing it to the character Gilfoyle from *Silicon Valley*—because it can actually push back and say no. Whenever he tries shortcuts with other models, he inevitably regrets it.

### The Police Script and Self-Correcting Loops [03:10](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=M1i11zsqsT8&t=190s)
Kitze's core methodology is his "police script"—a custom rule layer beyond what linters and TypeScript can catch. Every time he gets frustrated by an agent mistake, he adds a rule to the police file. After each agent turn, an automated loop runs TypeScript checks, the linter, the formatter, and finally the police check. If the police check finds 30 errors, it launches sub-agents to correct each one. The goal is to end every turn with zero warnings and zero type problems. He stresses this loop must run on every turn, not weekly or monthly.

### A New Engineering Skill Set [05:19](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=M1i11zsqsT8&t=319s)
Kitze argues that engineering with agents is a completely new skill that most engineers resist learning. People were proud of their speed in Vim or traditional IDEs, but that ego holds them back. He puts his own past speed in perspective: "I'm fast, but I'm not faster than engineering with LLMs." The new skill is writing rule files, engineering loops, and building guardrails—not typing code at lightning speed.

### Letting Go of Old Identities [06:53](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=M1i11zsqsT8&t=413s)
The interviewer notes that engineers who were blazing fast in traditional workflows often struggle to pivot because they're proud of those skills. Kitze traces his own mindset to college, where he realized he cared about solving problems, not deep engineering optimization. He distinguishes between two camps: those who want to solve problems and those who want to engineer deeply. Both fell under the same job titles, but the deep engineers are now the most hurt by the AI shift.

### The Rude Awakening for the Industry [09:29](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=M1i11zsqsT8&t=569s)
Kitze delivers a blunt assessment that most people in the industry are "overpaid to fuck around," referencing LinkedIn's perks like dog groomers and multiple lunches. He believes the tech industry's sense of being "above" other professions is ending, and engineers need a rude awakening: their skills may not be as unique and valuable as they thought. The bar has risen from "know a bit of HTML and CSS" to needing real engineering knowledge combined with agent orchestration ability.

### The Playing Field and Accelerated Learning [13:09](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=M1i11zsqsT8&t=789s)
Both speakers agree there's an equal playing field when new models drop—everyone gets access simultaneously. The edge comes from engineering your loops and processes for high-quality outcomes. Kitze reflects that if he'd had even ChatGPT-3-level intelligence during college, he'd have learned 20× faster. The field is open for those who want to evolve, but the oldest rule in the industry holds: people don't want to change.

### Starting a New Project with Agent Skills [16:15](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=M1i11zsqsT8&t=975s)
Kitze shares that his skills files are reused across all 140 projects. When starting something new, he always points the agent to an existing codebase—either his own "zero to ship" template or a simpler React/Tailwind starter—so the agent clones a known structure rather than inventing its own stack. He never starts completely fresh. The interviewer adds the principle that code must remain simple and easy to change, and if you don't engineer for that, rewrites accelerate from years to months.

### The Sidebar-and-Chat Problem [18:53](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=M1i11zsqsT8&t=1133s)
Kitze vents about every agent orchestrator—Cursor, Copilot, and others—defaulting to the same sidebar-plus-chat UI. For someone juggling 140 projects, this doesn't scale. He's been trying to invent a fundamentally different "agentic OS" for work, and recently had a breakthrough with Fable, his sixth iteration of building a custom orchestrator. His rule: no left sidebar. He wants an interface designed for reviewing parallel agent work, not just chatting with one agent at a time.

### 300 Changes in 200 Repositories [20:59](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=M1i11zsqsT8&t=1259s)
When asked how he managed 300 changes across 200 repositories in a single day, Kitze admits he's still struggling with orchestration. He keeps returning to manually working in the Codex app with GPT-5.5 on "extra high." He's tried building orchestrators six times and keeps abandoning them for the tried-and-true Codex workflow. He's a self-described terminal hater who only tolerated CLI tools until the Codex desktop app arrived.

### Humans as the Bottleneck [23:06](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=M1i11zsqsT8&t=1386s)
Kitze identifies the core problem: humans are becoming the bottleneck. You have limited cognitive load and observation skills, and when agents run in parallel, you can't review everything. Codex's UI is visually messy—when you return after an hour, you can't easily tell what's in progress, what's blocked, or what needs review. He believes agents can do far more; we just haven't designed the right interfaces to let them and to help us review their work.

### The Need for Experimentation and Room to Innovate [24:08](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=M1i11zsqsT8&t=1448s)
Both speakers agree that leadership teams must give engineers room to experiment. We're engineering our own engineering workflows—a level of process innovation that didn't exist before. Previously, innovation was limited to CI/CD and metrics frameworks. Now there's genuine disruption, and every organization is figuring it out simultaneously. The challenge is distinguishing real value from marketing fluff.

### Cost Justification and the Skill Issue [26:10](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=M1i11zsqsT8&t=1570s)
Kitze dismisses complaints about agent costs. People balk at Fable charging $40/hour, but he notes he used to charge $150/hour as a consultant. If an agent with world-class intelligence, paired with good loops and processes, costs $30–40/hour and is actually useful, that's a bargain. However, if it's burning tokens going in circles, the problem is your process, not the model—a "skill issue."

### The Culling of Coasters [29:24](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=M1i11zsqsT8&t=1764s)
The interviewer observes that people who aren't curious or energized by this shift will lose their comfortable cushion. Kitze agrees, saying there was always more room for coasting in the past—people played PS4 in offices and produced the spaghetti codebases that LLMs were later trained on. Now, in organizations with closed loops and agent-assisted PR reviews, it's harder to ship slop. The environment is getting tighter: "either you're a fucking rock star or you're gone."

### Missing the Point on Loop Engineering [32:03](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=M1i11zsqsT8&t=1923s)
Kitze reacts to the narrative that engineering loops is too much work and people would rather write code themselves. He calls this completely missing the point: the more effort you put into refining workflows, the more they scale. The cost-benefit is enormous. He compares it to the old habit of refusing to stop and refactor—engineers wanted to keep going and "refactor later." The same resistance is now happening with loop engineering.

### Solo Engineers vs. Teams with Agents [33:38](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=M1i11zsqsT8&t=2018s)
Asked about solo engineers with agent swarms versus teams using agents, Kitze is glad he works solo. He's not a good orchestrator of humans and sees his path as being solo with as many agents as possible. For teams, he sees a benefit: agents in the middle can unblock projects by enforcing agreed-upon rules, removing the emotional friction of human disagreement. Once a pipeline is established, the next engineer can't argue—"we didn't invent it, it was the agent."

### Agents Flipping the Org Chart [34:42](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=M1i11zsqsT8&t=2082s)
Kitze predicts agents will crawl up the org chart—from peer-programming helper, to manager of a three-person team, to CTO-level positions. Humans will move into unblocking roles at lower levels, with very few people at the top directing top-level agents. He acknowledges there will be rebellion—"Molotov cocktails, setting data centers on fire"—but considers the shift inevitable. The interviewer connects this to a VP at Vercel's observation that the best engineers unblock themselves and then unblock others.

### Ego as the Enemy [36:15](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=M1i11zsqsT8&t=2175s)
Kitze reflects that ego at every level—seniors, juniors, interns—hurts processes. The best people to work with set aside personal opinions for the greater goal of the sprint, the month, and the company. Stubborn people who cling to "my degree, my knowledge, my opinions" create friction and, if they don't budge, become bottlenecks that teams must move past.

### Final Advice: Stick to One Provider [37:49](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=M1i11zsqsT8&t=2269s)
Kitze's number one advice for getting started: stick to one provider and one model. Resist the urge to switch every time a new model drops. People who stayed with Claude while everyone jumped to Codex for GPT-5.5 may be outshipping everyone because they've built deep, refined practices. He compares it to Angular developers who mastered their framework being more productive than those who jumped to React, then Svelte, then the next thing. Go deep, not shallow.
