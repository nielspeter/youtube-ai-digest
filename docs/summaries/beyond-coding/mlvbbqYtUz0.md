# What Senior Engineers do Differently (Vercel VP)

## TL;DR
Lindsey Simon, VP of Engineering at Vercel, explains how AI agents are flattening engineering hierarchies and making everyone "junior engineers again." He argues that career growth now comes from proactive unblocking, systematizing help, continuous feedback, and returning to learning fundamentals—especially writing and concise communication.

## Key Takeaways
- AI agents are consolidating specialized roles; the Valley trend is everyone becoming a "member of the technical staff."
- Junior engineers arriving today are often better prepared than experienced engineers were at the same stage, thanks to hackathons, internships, and builder-minded culture.
- The best engineers unblock themselves and others proactively, then systematize that help to scale their impact.
- IC and management tracks should be parallel, with equal ceiling—management isn't the only path to leadership.
- Feedback should be sought early and continuously; don't disappear into a cave expecting to emerge with something perfect.
- Writing is the most underrated skill for engineers—it forces concise thinking and makes your work interesting to others.
- Agents make it easy to ask questions without social friction, but you should still try to unblock yourself first and bring well-grounded questions to peers.
- Telemetry and social feedback are both essential signals; if a user seems "wrong," you likely failed to communicate well.
- Career leveling maps to increasing scope of impact—self, team, multi-team, company, industry.
- A liberal arts resurgence matters: if English is the most important programming language, learn to write and distill information effectively.

## Detailed Breakdown

### FOMO and Proving Yourself [00:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mlvbbqYtUz0&t=0s)
Simon opens by acknowledging he has felt FOMO his entire life, framing it as a drive to prove himself wrong. He recounts interviewing at Google in 2006 with no expectations of belonging, getting the job, and then feeling incompetent among talented peers. His response was to fight rather than freeze—a mindset that has defined his career.

### Role Consolidation and the "Member of Technical Staff" Trend [02:09](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mlvbbqYtUz0&t=129s)
Asked whether AI will eliminate roles, Simon sees consolidation rather than disappearance. He notes the Valley trend of calling everyone a "member of the technical staff," driven partly by anti-poaching and partly by genuine flattening of responsibilities. Engineers can now engage in design, product, and other areas where they lack formal training, using agents to fill knowledge gaps.

### Early-Career Engineers Exceling in a Builder Culture [04:16](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mlvbbqYtUz0&t=256s)
Simon pushes back on the narrative that juniors are doomed. He sees new grads arriving with three years of hackathons and internships, sometimes more relevant experience than mid-career engineers. Their lack of "experience burden" makes them bolder—they don't hesitate to try things outside their expertise.

### Agents, Collaboration, and Asking Better Questions [06:29](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mlvbbqYtUz0&t=389s)
Simon observes that post-ChatGPT interns onboard effectively by first asking agents questions, then bringing well-grounded questions to humans when the AI's answers feel generic. This shift improves the quality of peer collaboration because you arrive having already tried to unblock yourself and can articulate why you're unsatisfied.

### Questioning and Building Fundamental Understanding [08:05](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mlvbbqYtUz0&t=485s)
The conversation turns to the skill of questioning answers rather than accepting them. Simon emphasizes digging backward into fundamental understanding, comparing it to evaluating source quality in search. He notes his daughter is already learning source evaluation in elementary school—a skill today's new grads bring to the workforce.

### "We're All Junior Engineers Again" [09:42](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mlvbbqYtUz0&t=582s)
Simon reflects on how agents make even experienced leaders feel like beginners again. The tools are newer than anyone's tenure in the industry. Rather than hiding from that FOMO, he leans into the craft of building, which is what drew everyone to engineering in the first place.

### IC vs. Management Tracks and Fulfillment [10:47](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mlvbbqYtUz0&t=647s)
Simon explains that Vercel deliberately separates management from leadership and keeps IC and management tracks parallel with equal ceilings. He shares his own experiment with management to see if he could scale impact beyond his personal PR throughput. Fulfillment shifted from the adrenaline of landing PRs to the enablement of watching others succeed.

### Building for Engineers and Taking Feedback to Heart [14:55](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mlvbbqYtUz0&t=895s)
Engineers are fickle, but Simon values both social signal (X, feedback) and telemetry (what actually saves time). He insists that if a user seems wrong, the company failed to communicate. He describes the motivating, sometimes painful experience of watching users struggle with your product in real time.

### Expectations for Senior and Staff Engineers [18:36](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mlvbbqYtUz0&t=1116s)
Simon doesn't think core expectations have changed meaningfully, though speed baselines have risen. He maps career levels to scope of impact: competent IC, tech lead, multi-team tech lead, company-level, and industry-level. What has changed is that agents compress certain tasks dramatically, shifting time expectations.

### What the Best Engineers Do Differently [20:46](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mlvbbqYtUz0&t=1246s)
The best engineers unblock themselves and others proactively. They systematize their help—leaving breadcrumbs so that helping two people directly scales to helping ten. Simon cites a Vercel engineer who codified years of React debugging experience into a shareable skill, extending his impact to industry level.

### Conferences and Returning to Learning Fundamentals [22:21](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mlvbbqYtUz0&t=1341s)
Simon appreciates the high-fidelity, personal conversations that only happen in person. He notices a renewed appetite for learning how to learn effectively—going back to fundamentals, closing knowledge gaps, and building on stronger foundations rather than rushing forward with splinters.

### Writing as the Most Important Skill [25:35](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mlvbbqYtUz0&t=1535s)
Simon advocates a resurgence of liberal arts education, arguing that if English is the most important programming language, engineers must learn to write concisely. Distilling information is especially critical when using AI, where you can burn tokens chasing answers. Symbolic logic and concise representation of patterns remain powerful.

### Final Takeaway: Seek Feedback Early [27:11](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mlvbbqYtUz0&t=1631s)
Simon's closing advice: don't disappear into a cave hoping to emerge with something earth-changing. Great practitioners develop by continuously getting feedback. His most important career advice was to start writing about what you're doing—it forces you to distill experiments into concise descriptions that interest other people.
