# Why Applying to Jobs No Longer Works

## TL;DR
The tech job market in 2026 is highly competitive, with over 120,000 layoffs and a disconnect between companies struggling to find "right" talent and candidates struggling to get replies. Career and recruitment expert Keki Mwaba explains that simply applying to jobs no longer works; instead, tech professionals must build a visible personal brand on LinkedIn, cultivate genuine relationships, and showcase a portfolio of work to stand out in an AI-saturated hiring landscape.

## Key Takeaways
- The tech job market has contracted significantly compared to the pandemic boom, with over 120,000 tech layoffs in 2026 alone (roughly 1,000 people a day).
- Despite high layoffs, companies still struggle to find talent because many initial layoffs were cost-saving measures that were later reversed when companies realized AI couldn't fully replace human workers.
- LinkedIn remains the top hiring platform, but treating it as a static "digital CV" is a missed opportunity; it should be used as a portfolio to showcase work, results, and personality.
- The type of engineering roles in demand has shifted from traditional backend/frontend roles to AI-specific engineering, and 66% of Microsoft hiring managers would not hire someone with zero AI skills.
- Middle management roles are shrinking, while companies are increasingly looking for highly technical individual contributors who can execute and deliver.
- Building relationships through thoughtful LinkedIn commenting and networking is often more effective than blindly submitting hundreds of job applications.
- CVs have become "too good" and overly tailored, making candidates look identical on paper; referrals and genuine relationships are now the primary way to stand out.
- Professionals should always prepare for potential layoffs by keeping their CVs updated, knowing their legal rights, consulting a labor lawyer, and maximizing company benefits before leaving.
- Portfolios don't need to be massive finished products; showing your thinking process (like a UX redesign concept or a response to a public outage report) can be highly effective.
- Burnout is a real risk for mid-career professionals with family responsibilities who feel pressured to constantly stay updated on rapidly changing AI technologies.

Keki Mwaba explains that the 2026 job market is significantly tougher than the pandemic boom or 2023, particularly in tech. While sectors like nursing and elderly care have an abundance of jobs, software engineers, product managers, and designers face fewer opportunities and higher competition. The type of engineer in demand has shifted from traditional backend, Android, and iOS roles to AI-specific engineers. Patrick notes that 2026 has seen over 120,000 layoffs—about 1,000 a day—largely at bigger tech companies, though smaller AI companies like OpenAI are still growing.

### The Layoff and Hiring Paradox [02:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=u9pVRdtNsQo&t=160s)
Addressing the paradox of 60,000 open software engineering vacancies alongside mass layoffs, Keki suggests many companies initially conducted layoffs for cost savings. Some companies even rehired laid-off workers after realizing AI couldn't fully replace human engineers, designers, and product managers. This has created an accelerated wave of layoffs followed by rehiring. Patrick highlights the shift in recruiter-engineer dynamics; engineers who were once in high demand now apply to hundreds of jobs without receiving a single reply, making the market feel disheartening.

### Treating LinkedIn as a Platform, Not a Digital CV [04:19](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=u9pVRdtNsQo&t=259s)
LinkedIn remains the number one hiring platform in tech, but Keki stresses that users are wasting it if they treat it merely as a digital CV. In the past, recruiters spammed engineers, but now engineers must create their own visibility by treating themselves like a product. The best-performing posts showcase learnings, failures, and personal projects. Patrick admits he struggles to find the time and incentive to post consistently while comfortable in his current role, but Keki warns that waiting until a layoff happens to build a network is often too late.

### The Power of Comments and Networking [08:06](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=u9pVRdtNsQo&t=486s)
For those short on time, Keki recommends limiting LinkedIn scrolling to 5–10 minutes a day and focusing on contributing rather than consuming. She shares a case study of a senior software engineer who transitioned to a manager role solely by leaving insightful comments on the posts of target companies' engineering managers. By the time he reached out directly, the managers already recognized his name, allowing him to advance his career without sending a single job application. Patrick notes that this approach shifts the framing from "commenting" to "driving conversation."

### AI's Impact on LinkedIn Authenticity [10:47](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=u9pVRdtNsQo&t=647s)
Patrick asks about the trend of using AI to bombard LinkedIn with content and robotic comments. Keki acknowledges the platform can feel spammy and notes that users are increasingly being called out for AI-generated comments. LinkedIn's algorithm has adapted to combat bots, shifting focus away from raw likes and toward metrics like saves, sends, and read time to measure post authenticity. While Patrick shares his love-hate relationship with the platform, he has noticed a positive increase in people actively working on their personal brands compared to past years.

### Preparing for a Layoff [14:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=u9pVRdtNsQo&t=840s)
Keki advises professionals to "never feel safe" and to always be prepared for a layoff. Practical steps include keeping a great CV, tracking your work, and building a personal portfolio (without sharing confidential company information). She highly recommends knowing your employment rights based on your country, understanding contract clauses like non-competes or garden leave, and having a one-hour consultation with a labor lawyer in advance to save thousands during package negotiations. Additionally, she suggests maximizing company benefits like health and dental insurance before a layoff potentially locks you out of the system.

### Navigating Layoff Morale and Career Shifts [16:02](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=u9pVRdtNsQo&t=962s)
Layoffs severely impact company morale, even for those who remain, creating anxiety about salary matching and job market flooding. Keki shares a story of a client who successfully scaled AI adoption at a company, only to realize she had automated herself out of a job. Instead of finding another full-time role, she pivoted to AI consultancy, earning a higher income. Keki also notes that laid-off big tech employees are increasingly moving to smaller, less well-known companies, which can offer more autonomy and enjoyable projects if they let go of their ego.

### The Problem with "Too Good" CVs [18:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=u9pVRdtNsQo&t=1120s)
Keki points out that CVs have become "too good" and overly tailored to job descriptions, causing candidates to look identical on paper. When interviewed, many struggle to speak deeply about the projects they claim to have built. To break through the noise, old-fashioned relationships and referrals are essential. Keki heavily encourages students and recent graduates to start building job search strategies and attending networking events during their studies, as universities rarely teach these softer networking skills.

### The Evolution of Engineering Roles [22:49](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=u9pVRdtNsQo&t=1369s)
Software engineers will still have a place in the future, but they must shift their skills to stay updated. Keki shares an anecdote about a staff-level backend engineer who decided to become a plumber. The engineer used AI to code 90% of the time, felt bored, and wanted a manual job that would likely remain unchanged in the near future. Patrick notes that companies are betting on smaller, more senior teams using AI tools to increase productivity rather than hiring large volumes of engineers, making the market highly competitive.

### AI in Job Descriptions and the Decline of Middle Management [30:35](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=u9pVRdtNsQo&t=1835s)
There has been a 70% increase in job descriptions mentioning AI-related technologies, even for non-engineering roles like marketing. A recent study showed 66% of Microsoft hiring managers would not hire someone with zero AI skills. Patrick predicts a shrinking need for engineering managers, arguing that if AI allows everyone to build, companies need more executors and fewer managers. Keki confirms this trend, noting that middle management is facing significant layoffs and that even manager roles now require deep technical expertise rather than just people management skills.

### Burnout Risks for Experienced Professionals [34:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=u9pVRdtNsQo&t=2060s)
Patrick raises concerns about the pressure to stay updated on rapidly evolving tooling while managing full-time jobs and personal responsibilities. Keki identifies two groups: those who are comfortable staying reasonably updated without overexerting, and those who try to do it all. The latter group, especially those with families and mortgages, is at a massive risk of burning out. She emphasizes the importance of self-care, leaning on support systems, and learning informally through diverse teammates to avoid tedious after-hours studying.

### Step-by-Step Guide to Landing Frontier AI Lab Jobs [37:30](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=u9pVRdtNsQo&t=2250s)
Keki outlines a strategy for engineers wanting to work at companies like OpenAI or Anthropic:
1. Ask yourself *why* you want to work there (brand vs. genuine product interest).
2. Do deep research into how the company builds using open-source and public information.
3. Connect with current or former employees for conversations and potential referrals.
4. Study their tech stack and learn the necessary skills.
5. Showcase relevant projects on your CV with clickable links, even if they are just prototypes.

### Crafting Authentic Outreach Messages [39:36](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=u9pVRdtNsQo&t=2376s)
Patrick wonders how to reach out to employees at target companies without seeming fake or desperate. Keki advises taking a data-driven approach, noting that the LinkedIn response rate is currently around 20%. She recommends personalizing the hook—such as mentioning a talk they gave on YouTube—rather than just asking for a job. Tracking different message templates and their response rates helps engineers refine their approach and overcome the fear of rejection.

### Building an Effective Portfolio [42:13](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=u9pVRdtNsQo&t=2533s)
For engineers struggling with analysis paralysis around building a grand portfolio, Keki suggests showcasing your thinking process even if you don't have a finished product. She gives an example of a UX designer who wanted to transition to retail by creating a mock redesign of the Adidas app. For software engineers, walking through the steps of a challenge, the solution, and the monitoring process is highly effective. Patrick suggests analyzing public outage reports as a way to demonstrate problem-solving skills to target companies.

### Final Advice on Personal Branding [45:23](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=u9pVRdtNsQo&t=2723s)
Keki's final advice is to not be afraid of being seen. Professionals should showcase their work, tag teammates, and share advice without worrying about looking "too thirsty." She recommends following thought leaders like Gergely Orosz of *The Pragmatic Engineer* for inspiration. The goal is to start small and find an organic, authentic way to build visibility rather than trying to become an influencer overnight.
