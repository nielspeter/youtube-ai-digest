## TL;DR
Nebius CTO Danila Shtan explains how the AI cloud provider hires generalist engineers through a unique "bootcamp" team-matching process, why agentic coding tools are powerful but overhyped, and which engineering skills—product thinking, decomposition, and algorithmic fundamentals—will remain durable as automation reshapes the profession.

## Key Takeaways
- Nebius is an AI-centric cloud provider built around massive GPU access, though Shtan believes the "AI cloud" niche will blur into general cloud computing within five years.
- The company hires strong generalist engineers without requiring prior AI experience, then runs a multi-month "bootcamp" where new hires rotate through teams before mutually choosing a permanent match.
- Shtan bans the word "headcount" internally; teams hire based on demand and backlog size, not fixed slots.
- AI-assisted interviews are explicitly rejected because cheating tools are too sophisticated and algorithmic coding sessions remain a reliable baseline signal of computer science competence.
- The "agents will do everything" promise is "bullshit"—Shtan sees agentic tools as powerful augmentations that require a skilled human driver, comparable to working with a junior engineer.
- Engineers who are purely task-oriented and mechanical (e.g., banking Java developers automating internal processes) are the cohort most at risk from AI automation.
- The most future-proof engineers possess product thinking, decomposition skills, and the ability to challenge AI output with a strong "bullshit meter."
- Nebius restricts access to hyped tools like Claude Code until guardrails exist to audit the full agent trajectory behind every pull request.
- CTO at scale is a management role focused on expectation management, team dynamics, and bridging engineering with business—not day-to-day technology.
- Algorithms remain in the interview process because they signal fundamental computer science understanding, which matters for infrastructure work where APIs and JSON are not the whole story.

## Detailed Breakdown

### Nebius Origin and AI Cloud Positioning [00:30](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zTenuG5b4Eo&t=30s)
Nebius did not exist four years ago, and its current shape as an AI-oriented cloud emerged roughly two and a half years ago. The company entered the general cloud market late and had to find a niche after ChatGPT and Llama accelerated AI demand. Shtan considers the "AI cloud" label somewhat artificial and predicts it will blur back into general cloud computing within five years. Earlier entrants in the AI cloud space included CoreWeave, Lambda Labs, and Crusoe, many of which shifted away from pure cloud toward large-scale facilities.

### Customers and Engineering Demand [03:41](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zTenuG5b4Eo&t=221s)
Nebius has engaged with every major AI lab, some of which are customers, along with AI-friendly enterprises that run significant AI workloads in the background. Shtan notes the company is proud of publicly disclosed customers listed on its website but is cautious about naming others.

### Infrastructure Engineering Skills in Demand [04:11](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zTenuG5b4Eo&t=251s)
Nebius hires general infrastructure engineers who understand distributed systems and scale without requiring prior AI experience. The company faces extreme difficulty being among the first to adopt new Nvidia chips in production, creating demand for systems programmers who can debug drivers and hardware-level issues. Low-level storage and networking engineers are critical because AI workloads require immense throughput—customers routinely download hundreds of gigabytes per second during training. With the industry shift from training to inference, GPU kernel programmers are among the highest-paid and most scarce talent, with Shtan estimating only a few hundred top-tier specialists worldwide.

### Hiring Process and the Bootcamp Model [06:52](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zTenuG5b4Eo&t=412s)
The hiring process is largely standard: coding sessions, written assignments, and systems design interviews, with specialized sections for deep expertise candidates. Notably, Nebius extends offers to join the company rather than a specific team. New hires enter a "bootcamp" lasting roughly three months, spending three to four weeks per team across several teams, participating in daily standups and day-to-day work. At the end, both the hire and the teams express preferences, and matches are made. Shtan personally conducts exit interviews for every bootcamp participant, assessing onboarding efficiency and team fit. Mismatches are rare and handled openly; serious mismatches can result in failed probation.

### No AI-Assisted Interviews [11:07](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zTenuG5b4Eo&t=667s)
Nebius does not allow agentic tooling in interviews. Shtan cites the sophistication of cheating tools as a core problem—interviewers cannot detect whether a candidate is receiving assistance even when watching them. The company is moving back toward onsite interviews for senior candidates, flying people in from other countries. Algorithmic coding sessions, while criticized as disconnected from production work, serve as a strong baseline signal of computer science fundamentals. Shtan acknowledges the company is designing a specialized "agent coding session" to assess how candidates interact with AI agents, but it is not yet in use.

### Hiring Philosophy and Headcount [16:28](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zTenuG5b4Eo&t=988s)
Nebius never stops hiring and processes roughly 10 to 15 engineers per month. Shtan spent half a year fighting to eliminate the word "headcount" from internal vocabulary—teams talk about demand, tasks, priorities, and backlog size instead. Teams preview candidate CVs and interview results to request specific bootcamp participants. The engineering organization has grown from about 300 to nearly 400 people, primarily based in Amsterdam with presence in London, Israel, the US, and remote across Europe.

### CTO as a Management Role [22:46](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zTenuG5b4Eo&t=1366s)
Shtan argues that beyond a certain scale, CTO is a management position, not a technical one. The role involves building teams, ensuring delivery, mediating between engineering and product or business functions, and structuring hiring and onboarding. A successful CTO must be a people person, a good manager, and an advocate for both engineering and business sides. The most critical management skill across modern companies is managing expectations—failures typically stem from poor expectation management driven by ego, pride, fear of seeming incompetent, or imposter syndrome.

### Agentic Engineering: Hype vs. Reality [28:37](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zTenuG5b4Eo&t=1717s)
Shtan describes constant internal debate with Nebius's CEO, who is bullish on AI hype. While acknowledging that models like Claude Sonnet 4.5 and GPT-5-class systems are genuinely capable of serious engineering work with the right agentic loops, Shtan rejects the "agents will do everything" promise and the "software factory" or "dark factory" concept. Autonomous agent loops in software engineering consistently fail without human direction. He compares the current AI coding hype to past hype cycles like JetBrains' ReSharper, which was revolutionary at launch but eventually became just another commodity tool.

### The Multiplication Problem with AI Agents [32:26](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zTenuG5b4Eo&t=1946s)
AI tools multiply a baseline skill level, but that baseline is not zero—it sits around mid-level to senior engineer competence. Below that baseline, multiplication amplifies incompetence, producing catastrophic results. Shtan cites examples of people creating startups with Claude Code in an hour, only to face immediate security crises because no competent engineer was in the loop. Models cannot invent knowledge outside their pre-training; they implement whatever is in the repository or prompt and report success regardless of quality. The "software factory" argument that autonomous AI is safe because code is formally verified by tests falls apart when the same model writes both implementation and tests, creating self-referential loops without external challenge.

### Working with Agents Is Like Working with a Junior Engineer [35:02](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zTenuG5b4Eo&t=2102s)
For experienced engineers like Shtan, agentic tools are transformative for prototyping—he can drop ideas into code immediately instead of scheduling planning sessions. However, working with an AI agent resembles working with a junior engineer: they lack full context, pursue odd ideas, require constant external challenge, and demand a strong internal "bullshit meter" to catch incorrect assertions. Shtan argues that engineers who thrive with agents often possess the skills of good engineering managers: decomposition, challenging assumptions, and understanding context beyond the immediate task.

### Tooling Access and Guardrails [37:08](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zTenuG5b4Eo&t=2228s)
Nebius provides access to OpenAI models and Codex, allowing engineers to build their own harnesses, but does not broadly distribute Claude Code. Shtan distinguishes between practical tool use and hype-driven demand—people want Claude Code because it is trendy, not because they know what to do with it. He has posted an internal manifesto requiring that every pull request merged into production clearly distinguish whether code was human-written or agent-assisted, and if agent-assisted, the full session trajectory must be available for audit. The review focus shifts from discussing code to discussing the thinking behind the code: what architecture was referenced, how the model was steered, and whether the engineer challenged it sufficiently.

### Technical Due Diligence Interview [40:49](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zTenuG5b4Eo&t=2449s)
For senior candidates, Nebius conducts a free-form "technical due diligence" interview about a recent project. This interview does not assess architecture or code quality but evaluates the candidate's understanding of the project's context: how requirements were gathered, who made decisions, why the project existed, what users thought, and how feedback was internalized. Shtan sees this as one of the most important qualities in modern engineers—people who think beyond their immediate task about the product and system as a whole.

### The At-Risk Cohort of Engineers [47:09](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zTenuG5b4Eo&t=2829s)
Shtan identifies a specific cohort at risk: engineers who perform mechanical, task-oriented work such as automating internal banking processes in Java. These roles involve no product thinking, no operations responsibility, and no broader system awareness. He predicts this cohort will vanish within three to five years as AI automates their work and no new engineers enter those career paths. However, he notes that job security through undocumented institutional knowledge will persist as a separate organizational problem.

### Build It, Own It, Run It [50:23](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zTenuG5b4Eo&t=3023s)
Nebius operates a full responsibility model: teams design, build, run, and are on-call for their systems, including Christmas night at 3 a.m. There are no architecture committees or separate architect roles. Shtan acknowledges this does not offer the best work-life balance but attracts people willing to take full ownership. He insists that production code for systems generating hundreds of millions in annual revenue must be owned by humans—agent-driven autonomous commits are non-negotiable at current capability levels.

### Where Agents Excel: Incident Response and Fact-Checking [52:33](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zTenuG5b4Eo&t=3153s)
Agents are genuinely useful in structured environments. During production incidents, agents can find root causes and mitigation plans in three minutes versus twenty for a human, because they can rapidly explore Kubernetes, logs, and metrics. Agents also excel at cross-referencing and fact-checking postmortem documents against Slack, monitoring, and Kubernetes data. However, agents are poor at writing postmortems themselves—their strength is in grounding and verifying facts, not synthesizing narrative. Humans conserve energy and skip details; agents do not, making them valuable for catching slips.

### Why Algorithms Stay in Interviews [55:18](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zTenuG5b4Eo&t=3318s)
Shtan keeps algorithmic interviews because they separate people who are "into tech" from actual engineers. The goal is not memorizing specific algorithms but assessing algorithmic thinking—knowing when to cut corners, understanding low-level primitives, and possessing fundamental computer science erudition. For infrastructure companies like Nebius, not all software is moving JSON between APIs; someone must build the systems underneath. Until another reliable baseline signal emerges, algorithms remain the best proxy for that understanding.
