## TL;DR
Stanford's STORM research method uses multiple AI agent perspectives to produce more organized, verified research articles than single-prompt approaches. The video demonstrates a free Claude skill that implements STORM by deploying five expert personas (practitioner, academic, skeptic, economist, historian), mapping their contradictions, and adversarially peer-reviewing the output into a reliable HTML briefing.

## Key Takeaways
- Stanford's STORM method produces articles 25% more organized than the next best method in peer-reviewed testing.
- The approach uses five expert perspectives—practitioner, academic, skeptic, economist, and historian—each finding blind spots the others miss.
- A four-prompt pipeline chains together: generating perspectives, mapping contradictions, synthesizing, and adversarial peer review.
- The final output is a self-contained HTML briefing with a 60-second summary, key findings ranked by reliability, and sources marked as confirmed, corrected, or demoted.
- Compared to Claude's native deep research (100+ agents), the STORM skill ran ~12 agents, was faster, 100% cheaper, and avoided API rate limits.
- An independent evaluation by Codex rated the STORM HTML briefing superior in evidence quality, source diversity, thesis strength, actionability, risk control, and content suitability.
- The skill can be tailored to your business context and customized with additional lenses (e.g., a sixth perspective for customer or frontline employee viewpoint).
- Subagents work under one main session and cannot communicate with each other, unlike agent teams/councils which can debate among themselves.
- The broader lesson is about borrowing subject matter expertise through agents to eliminate blind spots, rather than any single skill being universally optimal.
