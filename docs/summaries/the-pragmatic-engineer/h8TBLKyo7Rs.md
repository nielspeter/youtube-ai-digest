## TL;DR
Simon Ericson, founder and CEO of Turbopuffer, traces his journey from a self-taught Danish teenager who landed a job at Shopify, through eight years of deep infrastructure work, to building Turbopuffer — a search/vector database that stores data in S3 and runs on CPUs. The conversation covers his "napkin math" approach to performance reasoning, how Cursor became his first customer, his unconventional approach to venture capital, and the CPU scarcity now driven by reinforcement learning workloads.

## Detailed Breakdown

### Early Computing Journey [00:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=h8TBLKyo7Rs&t=0s)
Simon's introduction to computers came through PowerPoint, where he discovered that clickable diagrams could navigate between slides, effectively making PowerPoint Turing complete. He progressed through Microsoft FrontPage (which only worked in Internet Explorer), then discovered the underlying HTML, and began cobbling together snippets from the internet. He eventually upgraded to Dreamweaver, learned PHP, and hit a wall when he exhausted Danish-language programming resources online at around age 11–12. He then spent four years addicted to World of Warcraft, which dramatically improved his English — enabling him to re-engage with the broader English-speaking programming community.

### Competitive Programming and the IOI [02:05](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=h8TBLKyo7Rs&t=125s)
An internet friend in Australia told Simon about the International Olympiad in Informatics (IOI). He found the Danish qualifying process on a obscure website and applied. The problems were algorithmic in nature — completely different from his HTML/PHP background — involving optimization challenges like assigning packages to trucks (NP-complete problems where you compete on producing the best solution). This experience gave him confidence that he could sit down, read a paper, and figure things out through sustained effort.

### Joining Shopify Without a CS Degree [03:41](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=h8TBLKyo7Rs&t=221s)
Simon wrote an article about switching from an iPhone to a Nokia brick phone in 2013, which went viral on Hacker News and was featured by the New York Times. A Shopify recruiter connected the dots and reached out. Simon had a great call, was invited to Ottawa, and interviewed on-site — though Shopify likely didn't realize he was still in high school. He told them he needed to finish high school first, then moved to Canada in 2013. He initially planned a gap year but stayed for eight years, finding that the IOI experience had already taught him he could learn computer science concepts independently.

### Self-Taught Deep Dive at Shopify [05:47](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=h8TBLKyo7Rs&t=347s)
Feeling insecure about lacking a formal CS education, Simon adopted a rigorous self-teaching method: every time he heard a term he didn't know at work, he wrote it down and researched it that evening. He went deep on each concept — not just surface-level understanding but reading specifications, examining Wireshark traces, and studying the underlying protocols. He now looks for this same instinct to "peel back the layers" when interviewing engineers. This curiosity naturally pulled him toward the infrastructure team, where people were "closest to the metal."

### Scaling Shopify Infrastructure [08:22](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=h8TBLKyo7Rs&t=502s)
Simon described Shopify's rapid growth in the 2010s (120–140% year-over-year), preparing annually for Black Friday surges with physical hardware orders. Application-layer scaling problems consistently landed at the database layer. He worked on sharding (noting his boss Camilo's maxim "you can't cache writes"), multi-data-center deployments, and decomposing a mysterious 128GB Redis server that nobody understood until it went down. He built a failure-mode matrix specifying how each service should behave when each dependency was down, and wrote the test suite for it.

### Toxyproxy: Simulating Database Failures [10:59](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=h8TBLKyo7Rs&t=659s)
To test failure handling without mocking, Simon proposed shelling out to GDB, attaching to the process, and closing the file descriptor to the database — simulating a real database failure through the entire stack. This evolved into "Toxyproxy," a layer 4/7 proxy between applications and databases (like MySQL) that could be commanded via API to make the database appear down, slow, or corrupted. This enabled the entire failure matrix to be tested in CI, uncovering tens of bugs in the MySQL driver and Rails' connection handling. Toxyproxy is reportedly still running in Shopify's CI system today.

### Leaving Shopify and the Napkin Math Project [14:07](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=h8TBLKyo7Rs&t=847s)
After eight years (2013–2021), Simon felt he needed novelty to keep learning. He had worked on caching, rewrote Shopify's storefront with Justine (now his co-founder), ran multi-data-center operations, and handled database scaling. His "napkin math" project — a GitHub table of hardware performance numbers (DRAM bandwidth, S3 round-trip latency, NVMe SSD bandwidth, EBS volume performance, costs per gigabyte across storage types and commitment levels) — grew from his frustration with teams making infrastructure decisions based on poor benchmarks. He would compute the theoretical cost of a query from first principles and compare it to benchmark results, often finding massive discrepancies caused by misconfigured benchmarks (e.g., distributed queries across 100 nodes inflating P99 latency).

### Obsessing Over Database Internals [18:15](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=h8TBLKyo7Rs&t=1095s)
Simon described a 24-hour obsession with the question of how many writes per second MySQL could sustain. Napkin math suggested ~1,000 (one f-sync per millisecond), but MySQL achieved ~10,000 on a modest box. He traced through BPF probes and eventually discovered batching: f-syncs operate on 4K blocks, and MySQL batches multiple writes into each f-sync. He found the answer in an obscure article written by someone in a small German town — quipping that "the entire internet runs on small towns in Bavaria."

### The Origins of Turbopuffer [20:19](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=h8TBLKyo7Rs&t=1219s)
Three factors converged to create Turbopuffer: (1) a painful experience with a traditional search database at Shopify that he couldn't get to match napkin math predictions, (2) the napkin math project giving him facility with theoretical hardware limits, and (3) leaving Shopify and doing "angel engineering" — joining friends' companies for equity instead of cash. At Readwise (a bootstrapped Canadian company), he built a recommendation engine that worked well but would cost $30K/month for all users — against their existing $5K/month total infrastructure spend. The economics didn't work because vectors were stored in DRAM.

### Building on S3: The First Version [23:55](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=h8TBLKyo7Rs&t=1435s)
Simon did back-of-the-envelope math on storing vectors in S3 with clustering. S3's P99 latency for 256–512KB objects is ~200ms, and navigating a tree structure means multiple sequential round-trips, so he had to minimize round trips. He spent the summer of 2023 iterating on approaches, got an end-to-end working version in July, rewrote it twice, and released in October 2023. The first version was deliberately the simplest possible implementation: cluster the vectors, store clusters as files in S3, store centroids in a separate file, and search by downloading centroids then downloading the N closest clusters. He didn't implement a proper LSM tree or even a caching layer — he just put an nginx reverse proxy with caching in front of S3, running on a single server in a tmux instance.

### Cursor as First Customer [28:02](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=h8TBLKyo7Rs&t=1682s)
Cursor reached out after Simon launched Turbopuffer on Twitter. At the time, Cursor was roughly eight people, and their founders had been exploring using S3 for KV caching — they understood the economics of keeping active codebases in RAM and cold data in object storage. Simon flew to San Francisco, showed up at their office, and found them struggling with PostgreSQL/autovacuum issues. He helped diagnose and fix those problems, building trust that if he understood databases well enough to fix theirs, he could build one. Cursor migrated to Turbopuffer within a couple weeks, and their bill dropped 95% — from the previous vendor's pricing to ~$4K/month, matching Simon's napkin math prediction.

### Meeting Jensen Huang at NVIDIA [34:44](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=h8TBLKyo7Rs&t=2084s)
At an NVIDIA HQ event, Simon introduced himself on stage by joking that if everything went wrong, Turbopuffer could pivot into vapes (referencing the name). Jensen Huang and NVIDIA leadership were in the room. Simon then couldn't stop talking about CPUs — praising AVX-512, SIMD, and the abundance of CPUs — despite his team's pre-event instruction to avoid mentioning the "C-word" (CPUs) around NVIDIA. He asked Jensen if he vapes; Jensen did not answer.

### CPU Scarcity and Cloud Dynamics [38:23](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=h8TBLKyo7Rs&t=2303s)
GPUs remain scarce, but CPUs are now also hard to get. Simon attributes this to reinforcement learning (RL) workloads: teaching models to search, use tools, run Bash, and learn from real environments requires massive CPU capacity. AI agents also run general-purpose workloads on CPUs. As AI becomes more applied, the feedback loop intensifies — models need more RL environments for domains where they're weak, driving further CPU demand. Turbopuffer competes for CPU and NVMe SSD allocations with its own customers. The company mitigates this by being able to run across many different machine SKUs (C4 forces on GCP, Z4Ds, ARM C4As) rather than requiring a specific instance type.

### Unconventional Approach to Venture Capital [43:03](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=h8TBLKyo7Rs&t=2583s)
Simon initially didn't know if Turbopuffer was a venture-scale opportunity and approached capital from first principles: revenue needed to exceed costs. He and Justine didn't take salaries for six months and personally funded GCP bills. When he wanted to hire Bojan (a former IOI competitor) and another engineer, he raised $700K from an individual named Locky — telling him that if the product didn't show product-market fit by year's end, he'd shut it down and return the money. This terrified some VCs but reflected Simon's philosophy of playing with open cards. Turbopuffer became profitable later that year.

### Six Reasons to Raise Capital [48:46](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=h8TBLKyo7Rs&t=2926s)
Simon enumerated six reasons to raise venture capital: (1) fund R&D, (2) fund growth, (3) founder ego — which he called dangerous and dilutive to employees, (4) reward employees with liquidity, (5) strategic partnerships, and (6) M&A. Turbopuffer has only raised for reasons one and four. He explicitly called out founder ego as a common but harmful motivation that inflates valuations, sets unrealistic expectations for future employees, and turns fundraising into a status game.

### Remote Culture: Campfires and Turbo Credits [51:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=h8TBLKyo7Rs&t=3080s)
Turbopuffer is fully remote by necessity — Simon believed only San Francisco and maybe New York had the talent density to build a database company in person, so he committed to distributed work. The company gathers twice a year for offsites (Berlin, Mexico City). "Campfires" are informal in-person gatherings: when a few people congregate somewhere, everyone is invited to join. Some employees attend every campfire; others only come to the biannual offsites. "Turbo Credits" reward extracurricular contributions (conference talks, blog posts, working an expo floor) with business-class flight upgrades, encouraging travel and in-person interaction. The credits are apparently evolving toward a internal economy with a central bank and interest rates.
